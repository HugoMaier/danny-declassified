The years around the euro cash changeover did not transform Germany through one dramatic price shock. They changed it more quietly: through a country that remained largely renter-based, through the sale of public housing, through high unemployment, and through a welfare reform agenda that explicitly aimed to raise employment and lower labor costs. Between 1997 and 2006, Germany did not simply modernize. It hardened.

A quieter break than public memory suggests

When people look back at the early 2000s in Germany, they often remember the euro, rising everyday costs, and the feeling that something familiar had started to slip. But the deeper shift of the period was not just monetary. It was social. In the decade from 1997 to 2006, Germany remained a country of tenants rather than owners, lived through years of mass unemployment, sold off significant public housing stocks, and reworked the logic of its welfare state. The result was not a sudden collapse. It was a quieter renegotiation of the social contract.

Housing is the best place to start, because it reveals how limited broad-based asset security still was. According to Destatis, the owner-occupied dwelling rate in Germany was 40.9% in 1998, 42.6% in 2002, and 41.6% in 2006. Germany therefore remained a renter-majority society throughout the period. At the same time, rents did rise: Destatis reported an average Bruttokaltmiete of EUR 408 in 2002, up 8% from the previous survey, and EUR 410 in 2006, equivalent to EUR 5.94 per square metre. Yet this was not a classic national housing-price boom. The Bundesbank later described the German housing market of that era as a special case, marked by an extended cycle with stable prices rather than the sharp booms and busts seen elsewhere.

Housing without broad-based security

That matters sociologically. A society in which homeownership remains limited is more dependent on the quality, affordability, and stability of rental housing. And precisely there, the public sector was becoming less protective. The official record is fragmented: the Bundestag's Wissenschaftliche Dienste noted that for housing privatization, Destatis brought no further insights and that BBSR was effectively the key source for systematic transaction data. BBSR in turn reported that 379,000 municipal dwellings were sold in large transactions between 1999 and mid-2011, with about 95,000 of them sold more than once. For the years close to this period, one of the clearest officially documented examples is Dresden: in March 2006, the Regierungspräsidium confirmed the sale of all shares in WOBA Dresden to Fortress for EUR 981.7 million, while binding the buyer to the city's social charter.

The same pattern appears at state level, even if there is no single uniform official table for all 16 Länder across 1997 to 2006. In Lower Saxony, later Landtag documents describe the former state-owned NILEG as having had over 30,000 dwellings before its sale. In other words, the issue was not only local. Across different parts of Germany, housing that had once functioned as a public or quasi-public buffer was increasingly treated as an asset to be sold, restructured, and financially optimized. That was a major social shift, even where headline prices stayed relatively calm.

The retreat of owner-occupation support

At the same time, the state was retreating from one of its main instruments for widening access to owner-occupation. The 1995 Bundestag papers on the Eigenheimzulage made the original intention explicit: the policy was meant to make access to owner-occupied housing easier, especially for households near the threshold of homeownership. The design was generous by later standards: up to 5,000 DM annually for new builds and up to 2,200 DM for acquisitions, generally over eight years, with an added child component. But this support was cut back sharply.

In 2003, Bundesrat material described the revised regime as one in which support would in future amount to at most EUR 1,250, with EUR 800 per child. Then, in late 2005, the Bundestag passed the law abolishing the Eigenheimzulage for new cases from 2006 onward. The political message was unmistakable: ownership remained desirable, but the public means of helping ordinary households into ownership were being reduced and then withdrawn.

Unemployment as background pressure

That withdrawal did not happen in a socially calm environment. Germany was carrying a heavy unemployment burden throughout the period. Destatis shows an unemployment rate of 11.4% in 1997, 9.4% in 2001, 9.8% in 2002, 11.7% in 2005, and 11.0% in 2006. In the new Länder including Berlin, the rate was far higher: 17.7% in 1997, 17.7% in 2002, 18.7% in 2005, and 17.3% in 2006. These are not background numbers. They explain why the political system was willing to accept reforms that would have seemed much harsher in a low-unemployment society.

Agenda 2010 and the change in the social model

That is the context in which Agenda 2010 has to be read. The Bundestag's own retrospective says the goal of the policy was to raise employment and lower labor costs in order to make Germany more competitive. Its labour-market core included more flexibility in temporary agency work and dismissal protection, easier rules for minor employment, shorter benefit duration for some older unemployed workers, and the reorientation of labour-market policy around Fördern und Fordern. Above all, unemployment assistance and social assistance were to be merged into a single system. The same Bundestag paper notes that the essential reforms were implemented between December 2002 and January 2005.

The sociological importance of that change is hard to overstate. The old model had offered more status-based protection: unemployment was bad, but it did not as quickly dissolve one's social standing. Agenda 2010 moved the system toward activation, conditionality, and pressure to re-enter the labour market. The Bundestag itself describes the package as one of weitreichende Strukturreformen aimed at an umfassende Modernisierung der sozialen Marktwirtschaft. In political language, that sounded like renewal. In everyday life, it meant that security became more conditional, and that the state increasingly expected adaptation from below.

There is also an important statistical caution. The jump in official unemployment in 2005 should not be read too mechanically as proof that the reform initially failed. DIW and IAB-linked work notes that Hartz IV widened the official definition of unemployment because former social-assistance recipients capable of work now had to register as unemployed to receive benefits. That means the 2005 numbers capture both real hardship and an administrative reclassification of hardship. Yet even that measurement issue reinforces the broader point: millions of people were not just observing a policy change from a distance. They were being redefined by the state in terms of entitlement, obligation, and employability.

Conclusion

Seen together, these developments form a coherent picture. Germany did not become a broad homeowners' republic. It remained a renter society. Rents rose, but not in the form of a nationwide speculative explosion. Public and quasi-public housing stocks were sold. Homeownership was politically praised while financial support for entering it was cut back and then ended for new cases. And unemployment policy was transformed in a way that explicitly linked social protection more tightly to labour-market discipline.

That is why the years from 1997 to 2006 deserve to be remembered not simply as the era of the euro changeover, but as a quieter turning point in the Federal Republic's social order. The public language of the time spoke of modernization, competitiveness, reform, and efficiency. But beneath that language, many households experienced something else: a world in which secure renting looked less protected, buying looked less attainable, unemployment remained widespread, and state support became more conditional. The decade did not destroy Germany's social model in one blow. It made it thinner.

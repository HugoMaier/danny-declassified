It is worth stating the key point clearly at the outset: bureaucracy reduction in Germany has worked better than its reputation suggests once you look at it without the added burden of EU-driven rules. If one considers only the nationally measured bureaucracy costs in the narrow sense — that is, the classic paperwork of applications, notifications, certificates, and documentation requirements — Germany has in fact achieved real progress since the mid-2000s. The poor overall impression arises mainly when one includes the much broader compliance burden and also takes into account that, in recent years, a large share of new burdens has come from the implementation of EU law.

The Past: Germany's Bureaucracy Reduction Was More Successful in Its Early Phase Than People Often Remember Today

The real systematic reduction of bureaucracy at the federal level began with the government program "Bureaucracy Reduction and Better Regulation" starting in 2006. At that time, the bureaucracy costs borne by businesses were measured systematically for the first time. The starting point was €49.3 billion in annual bureaucracy costs caused by information obligations. By the end of 2011, the political goal of reducing this burden by 25 percent had been achieved; the federal government put the relief at €12.3 billion per year. This was not a symbolic success, but a real and measurable reduction in nationally induced information obligations.

However, a problem already became visible at that stage, and it still shapes the German debate today: reducing new or old obligations is only part of the story. When Destatis later updated the database, the actual level of bureaucracy costs as of January 1, 2018 was once again €50.0 billion. The reason was not that the earlier bureaucracy reduction had been "wrong," but that labor costs, material costs, and case volumes had risen. In other words: even if the state streamlines rules, the execution of existing rules can still become more expensive. That explains why Germany's bureaucracy reduction often looks worse in hindsight than it actually was in its first phase.

Europe's role was relevant from the beginning, but not yet as dominant as it is today. Even in the early measurement system, information obligations arising from federal law and EU law were recorded together. So a significant share of the burden was never purely national. At the same time, the logic of bureaucracy reduction back then was still focused mainly on classic paperwork. That worked as long as the main target was forms, reports, certificates, and notification obligations.

The Present: Germany Has Made Progress on Paperwork, but Has Lost Ground on the Broader Compliance Burden

Today the picture is contradictory. The official Bureaucracy Cost Index stood at 96.36 at the end of 2025, compared with the base value of 100 in 2012. This means that, in the narrow sense of classic paperwork, the burden on businesses today is slightly lower than it was then. The Meseberg relief package and several digitalization measures have also had measurable effects. For example, the Bureaucracy Cost Index fell noticeably in the 2022/2023 reporting period, and the obligation to use e-invoicing alone produced estimated relief of around €1.36 billion.

At the same time, however, the real problem has shifted. The burden on many businesses, citizens, and public authorities no longer comes only from forms, but from the much broader compliance burden. This includes technical adjustments, retrofitting, additional inspections, new organizational duties, or complex implementation procedures. It is precisely here that the report "Better Regulation 2022/2023" shows the break: while the ongoing bureaucracy costs for businesses fell by almost €1.4 billion, the ongoing compliance burden increased significantly at the same time — by billions of euros for citizens, businesses, and the public administration combined.

The most important structural reason for this is the growing role of Europe. The 2022/2023 annual report explicitly states that the 1:1 implementation of EU requirements alone caused an additional ongoing compliance burden of €4.5 billion for businesses during the reporting period. The National Regulatory Control Council has also shown that, in recent years, a very large share of ongoing burdens has stemmed from EU law. National bureaucracy reduction was therefore not meaningless — but it was increasingly overshadowed by a level of regulation that Germany's national "one in, one out" logic long captured only incompletely.

This explains the paradox of the present: Germany is indeed reducing bureaucracy in some areas, while businesses and citizens often feel more burdened than before. Both can be true at the same time. Paperwork has been reduced in part; at the same time, the material burden of implementation and adjustment created by many rules has increased. Bureaucracy reduction in the old sense has therefore progressed, while regulatory costs in the broader sense have often continued to grow.

The Future: Less Bureaucracy Will Only Be Possible with a Different Architecture of the State

Anyone who wants to draw the right lesson from this should stop treating bureaucracy reduction as a collection of small relief laws. The decisive question for the future is no longer: Which form can be abolished? The decisive question is: How must the state be organized so that the same data are not requested multiple times, the same proof is not submitted multiple times, and the same process is not reviewed multiple times? That is precisely why register modernization is the most important lever. The National Regulatory Control Council estimates the potential of a functioning once-only and register system at around €6.3 billion per year.

A second major lever lies in tax law and documentation requirements. Back in 2020, the Federal Ministry for Economic Affairs stated that tax bureaucracy accounts for around 40 percent of total bureaucracy costs. Significant savings had already been estimated years ago for shorter retention periods; reducing them to five years was estimated to have an annual relief potential of almost €3.9 billion. Measures like these may sound less spectacular than grand reform rhetoric, but in everyday business life they could make a much bigger difference than the next political slogan.

Third, Germany must learn to legislate in a digital-ready way instead of merely digitalizing bad processes afterward. Experience so far with the electronic certificate of incapacity for work, e-invoicing, practical checks, and digital procedures shows that noticeable relief is possible when rules are designed from the beginning to be practical and digitally workable. So the direction is right wherever the state does not simply move analog bureaucracy onto a screen, but actually simplifies the underlying process.

Fourth, bureaucracy reduction in Germany will no longer work in the future without a European strategy. As long as Berlin reduces burdens nationally while new burdens arrive from Brussels or from the implementation of European rules, the balance will remain politically frustrating. Germany therefore needs earlier influence in EU legislation, stricter cost assessments, less burdensome 1:1 implementation, and a bureaucracy brake that no longer ignores EU-related burdens. The core problem today is no longer only Germany's own tendency toward regulatory zeal, but the missing coordination between national bureaucracy reduction and European lawmaking.

A Sober Forecast for the Next Ten Years

The realistic outlook is neither resigned nor euphoric. Germany can indeed significantly reduce bureaucracy costs and compliance burdens over the next ten years — but only if it moves from the logic of small repairs to a structural reform of implementation and administration. Based on officially identified potentials, an additional net relief in the upper single-digit to lower double-digit billions of euros per year by the mid-2030s appears plausible if register modernization, tax simplification, digital checks, an EU burden filter, and sector-specific procedural reforms are actually implemented. This is not an official forecast, but a cautious extrapolation based on published potentials and previously observed relief effects.

The real question for the future is therefore not whether Germany will pass yet another Bureaucracy Relief Act. The real question is whether the state can become simpler, more digital, and more strategically intelligent at the European level. If that succeeds, bureaucracy reduction in Germany may ultimately turn out to be more successful than its current reputation suggests. If it fails, Germany will still be celebrating relief laws ten years from now while complaining about rising burdens at the same time.

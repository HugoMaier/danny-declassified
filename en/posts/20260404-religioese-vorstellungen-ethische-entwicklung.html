The classic religion-critical question is this: If many images of God appear so strikingly human — wrathful, jealous, punitive, eager for reverence — then what is the human being supposed to take as a moral guide? One still influential answer comes from Ludwig Feuerbach: for him, religion often appears as projection. Human beings place their own wishes, fears, dependencies, and ideals into the heavens and then encounter them there as "God." In this view, problematic images of God are not accidental, but reflections of human constitution. (plato.stanford.edu)

Yet perhaps the problem lies even deeper. Religious images of God may be problematic not only because they seem too human, but also because we often understand the human being too naively. The human self clearly consists not only of crude private self-interest. It also includes needs for attachment, moral needs, desires for recognition, status interests, and the need for inner coherence. If that is so, then "social egoism" would not simply be a perversion of morality, but often its normal human basic form. That is precisely why it need not be automatically reprehensible. (plato.stanford.edu)

The self is more social than moral ideals often assume

The strong form of psychological egoism claims that every human being ultimately has only one highest goal: personal well-being. This thesis remains disputed in philosophy, and its standard definition already shows how strict it is. It claims not merely that self-interest always plays some role, but that it is always the final and proper end. (plato.stanford.edu)

Empirically, however, the human motivational system appears far more complex today. Recent work on helping behavior in natural settings no longer models prosocial decisions as a simple choice between egoism and altruism, but as a combination of social, affective, moral, and expectation-based motives. People often help because others are not indifferent to them, but also because belonging, reciprocity, self-image, and social consequences are part of the picture. This is not a moral catastrophe, but first of all a more realistic anthropology. (nature.com)

The World Happiness Report 2025 fits this picture as well. There, helping behavior is described as "twice-blessed": it often benefits both the recipient and the helper. The fact that moral behavior also benefits the person acting is not evidence against its moral worth. Much suggests that human morality becomes more stable when it does not constantly have to work against the basic structure of the self. (worldhappiness.report)

Social egoism is not the same as crude selfishness

If the self is socially and morally constituted, then it is not inherently wrong to act well out of an expanded form of self-interest. Someone who helps because fairness matters, because they want to experience themselves as decent, because relationships ought to be protected, or because reciprocity seems desirable is still acting in a self-related way — but not necessarily in a vulgar egocentric sense. Between raw selfishness and socially embedded self-interest lies a wide field of human motives. (plato.stanford.edu)

Research on reputation and cooperation supports precisely this middle view. Reputation effects can systematically promote cooperation; people respond to the reputation of others and reward cooperative partners. Thus prosocial behavior is often genuinely linked to social benefit. But that does not mean it is worthless. A mature ethics should not deny this, but understand it. (nature.com)

At the same time, this insight also has a darker side. Recent review work on "willful ignorance" shows that people sometimes avoid information about the consequences of their actions precisely in order to protect their moral self-image while still pursuing their own advantage. The moral self is not only a source of good action, but also a source of refined self-deception. (sciencedirect.com)

Why self-knowledge matters so much

Not the illusion of pure selflessness, but insight into one's own limitations may stand at the beginning of ethical development. A recent overview of self-knowledge emphasizes that self-knowledge is systematically limited precisely with regard to morally relevant traits such as fairness, honesty, and compassion. Human beings therefore have especially good reason not to be too certain of themselves in moral matters. (sciencedirect.com)

This insight is not depressing, but productive. Anyone who acknowledges their own mixed motives will tend to be more cautious about moral self-exaltation. Such a person will more readily seek rules, criticism, institutions, and countervoices instead of prematurely assuming themselves virtuous. Recent work on self-acceptance even suggests that honest self-acceptance may be positively related to prosocial action. (pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov)

Ethical progress probably does not begin where human beings overcome the self as though they were angels, but where they understand the self more realistically. Anyone who knows that they need recognition, are prone to self-deception, are morally limited, and yet remain capable of cooperation has a better chance of acting with both greater humility and greater responsibility. (sciencedirect.com)

What this means for religion

Religion becomes an ethical obstacle when it sanctifies the most immature sides of the self. If it presents wounded pride as holy wrath, group narcissism as chosenness, retaliation as justice, and the need for reverence as piety, then it not only mirrors the human being but ennobles precisely its most problematic impulses. In this form, religion does indeed hinder ethical development. Feuerbach's projection thesis retains great explanatory power here. (plato.stanford.edu)

But religion need not function in this way. It can process the same anthropological basic facts differently: not as the deification of one's own standpoint, but as a school of humility, duty, self-limitation, and the expansion of the moral horizon. The fact that people often follow rules even under more anonymous conditions, although breaking the rule would harm no one, shows that norm-following is not based solely on naked expectation of sanctions. This can be religiously abused, but it can also be ethically cultivated. (nature.com)

The decisive question, then, is not simply whether religion exists, but what form of self-interpretation it teaches. Does it tell human beings: "Your impulses are already holy because they are yours"? Or does it say: "Recognize your limitations, take them seriously, and subject them to stricter examination"? Only the second form would, from this perspective, have genuine ethical value. (plato.stanford.edu)

Conclusion

The more mature answer to the original question is therefore not that human beings must become completely selfless. More likely, that is neither psychologically realistic nor ethically necessary. What matters more is that human beings interpret their own motives less naively. Social egoism is not bad in itself; it becomes dangerous only when it fails to recognize itself and thereby disguises itself as pure virtue. (plato.stanford.edu)

Precisely here may lie the beginning of moral development: not in denying the self, but in soberly illuminating it. The human being remains a creature with needs for protection, meaning, recognition, attachment, and moral self-respect. If this structure is openly acknowledged, more humility, more truthfulness, and perhaps also a better ethics may emerge from it. The recognition of human limitation would then not be the end of moral hope, but its realistic beginning. (sciencedirect.com)

Why the Culture War Could Become So Bitter

Economic pressure, the refugee crisis, a shortage of affordable housing, and the symbolic overloading of identity-political debates

—

This analysis connects economic, social, and cultural factors. It views identity-political conflicts not as an isolated cause, but as an amplifier of already existing tensions.

Anyone seeking to understand why political and cultural conflict in Germany has become so sharp in recent years should be careful with buzzwords. The term "wokeness" is often used polemically and conceals more than it explains. Analytically more useful is to speak of a highly visible preoccupation with recognition, identity, language, and symbolic conflicts. Our research on globalisation, EU Eastern Enlargement, the housing market, and the rise of the AfD suggests that these conflicts did not arise in a vacuum. They struck a society in which economic openness, structural change, and socially unequally distributed burdens had already generated deep insecurities.

The material backdrop is decisive. Our research shows that Germany has overall benefited from globalisation and European integration since the late 1990s, but that gains were distributed unequally. Those under particular pressure were workers in routine-heavy, tradeable, and weakly protected occupations, while export-strong sectors, urban centres, and the highly qualified fared significantly better through this change. This fits the finding that the German middle class, according to the OECD, shrank from 70% to 64% of the population between 1995 and 2018. This was no marginal note, but a signal that for many people the old connection between work, stability, and upward mobility was becoming more fragile.

The housing question added to this. Our research makes very clear that political alienation runs not only through wages and jobs, but also through blocked life trajectories. Someone who works but cannot build up property nor move securely on a strained housing market experiences economic pressure not as an abstract statistic, but as a biographical dead end. The housing need in Germany remains high according to the BBSR at around 320,000 new homes per year until 2030, and Destatis reported a housing cost overburden of 12.0% of the population in 2024. In such a situation, distributional policy quickly becomes a question of dignity, belonging, and the future.

Against this already strained backdrop, the refugee crisis of 2015/16 acted as a political accelerant. Our research describes it not as a singular cause of the subsequent culture war, but as a moment in which diffuse insecurity and loss of control condensed politically. The BAMF registered 476,649 asylum applications in 2015 and as many as 745,545 in 2016. At the same time, the FES analysis of the 2025 federal election shows that the media debate was strongly dominated by asylum and refugee policy, while for individual voting decisions the economic situation, peace and security, and social justice were at least equally important, or even more so. This tension is central: migration became a highly visible symbol, but the underlying frustration was broader and deeper.

In this context, the strong public preoccupation with "woke" topics took on a particular effect. It was not the cause of rising rents, not the reason for industrial disruptions, and not the origin of political polarisation. But it could be read by burdened groups as a signal of priorities: while many people were struggling with job security, housing costs, status, and social stability, language questions, symbolic questions, and morally heavily coded identity debates were often at the forefront of public dispute. Under conditions of social vulnerability, such things are easily understood as a message that the cultural sensibilities of urban and academic milieus receive more attention than the everyday problems of the working lower-middle class. This is less a material than a symbolic mechanism — but politically it is enormously effective.

This is precisely where the question of recognition and status comes in. Studies on AfD support in Germany show that not only objective poverty, but also weak social integration, status anxiety, the feeling of a lack of appreciation, and political marginalisation can increase agreement with the far right. The Böckler Foundation also points out that economic worries, insufficient co-determination, and a lack of recognition at the workplace correlate with right-wing extremist attitudes. This fits closely with our research: the conflict was not only ignited by what people have, but also by whether they feel seen, respected, and represented in society. When economic vulnerability combines with cultural grievance, social frustration quickly becomes identity-political defensiveness.

Why did this become such a bitter culture war? Because such topics are ideally suited to the logic of modern polarisation: they are visible, morally charged, easily escalated digitally, and identity-related. The MIDEM Polarisation Barometer shows that immigration is perceived in Germany as the topic with the greatest divisive potential. The Science Barometer 2025 in turn shows, for the thematic areas it examined, that the discourse around gender-neutral language exhibits the highest measured affective polarisation. This is no contradiction, but points to something important: immigration bundles control and distributional conflicts, while language and identity questions are particularly prone to emotionalisation. When both coincide in an already irritated society, debates are no longer experienced as normal political disagreements, but as a battle for interpretive authority, respect, and cultural dominance.

It would nevertheless be too simple to hold the intensive preoccupation with identity-political themes blanket-responsible for the shift to the right. A more recent comparative study on Western Europe even concludes that left-wing parties do not automatically lose the support of workers when they place greater emphasis on cultural themes; under certain conditions, such an emphasis can even have a positive effect. In professional terms: the problem was probably not the mere existence of progressive cultural topics, but their effect in a situation where economic insecurity, housing stress, fears about industrial transition, and migration conflicts were already high. Only in this constellation could identity-political communication be read as a sign of social distance and moral instruction.

Our overall conclusion is therefore: the bitter sharpness of the culture war does not explain itself from "wokeness" alone. It arose where several crisis experiences overlapped: economic structural change, unequally distributed consequences of globalisation, pressure on lower labour market segments, a housing shortage, the refugee crisis as a symbol of loss of control, and finally strongly morally coded identity conflicts. The intensive preoccupation with such topics acted in this environment above all as an amplifier. It made visible a deeper conflict that was already there: the conflict over who bears the material burdens, who receives cultural recognition, and who in the democratic public still feels they belong to social normality. It is precisely for this reason that from a series of individual debates a comprehensive, emotionally charged culture war could emerge.

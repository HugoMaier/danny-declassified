The debate about raising the retirement age to 70 almost always begins with a demographic argument: Germany is ageing, life expectancy is high, and the pension system is under pressure. In fact, average life expectancy has now roughly returned to its pre-crisis level; for 2024, Destatis reports 83.5 years for women and 78.9 years for men. A person who reaches 65 can statistically expect to live another 20.9 years on average; for men, the figure is 17.7 years. At first glance, this seems to support the claim that working longer is only logical. But this average conceals how unevenly long life is distributed in Germany.

The decisive question is not whether people live longer on average. The decisive question is who lives longer, how healthy those additional years are, and whether people can realistically keep working until 70. This is where the social fault lines begin. Data from the Robert Koch Institute for 2020 to 2022 show a pronounced life expectancy gap between more affluent and more deprived regions: in the most disadvantaged regions, women live 4.3 years less and men as much as 7.2 years less than in the least disadvantaged regions. What often appears in the pension debate as a neutral average is therefore deeply shaped by social inequality.

The pattern becomes even clearer when income is taken into account. For people with high incomes, the pension system is more favourable because they not only build up larger entitlements, but also draw their pensions for longer on average. An analysis based on SOEP data shows that men in the highest income group can expect to live about 21.7 more years after 65, and women 22.8 more years. In households at risk of poverty, remaining life expectancy is more than six years lower for men and around four years lower for women. This means that a rigid retirement age quickly becomes regressive: those who earn little often live shorter lives and would be hit particularly hard by additional working years.

Occupational status also makes a major difference. In the same analysis, manual workers have clear disadvantages compared with civil servants, who have the highest remaining life expectancy: 5.6 fewer years for men and 3 fewer years for women. There is also a strong effect of occupational strain. Men in highly demanding occupations have a remaining life expectancy that is around four years lower than that of men in low-strain occupations; for women, the gap is about two years. A pension reform that treats everyone the same therefore affects people in very unequal life situations.

This is precisely why shift and night work matter so much. There is no official statistic for "life expectancy in three-shift occupations," but the available evidence clearly shows that shift work is not a marginal phenomenon. According to the BAuA working time survey, around 16% of all employees work shifts. Shift work is especially common in medical and non-medical healthcare professions at 43%, in manufacturing occupations at 37%, and in security occupations, where 27% work rotating shifts with night components or permanent night shifts. These jobs are central to the debate on longer working lives because they are physically, psychologically, and socially demanding.

The health data are sobering. Employees without shift work rate their health as good or very good much more often, namely 71%. Among those in rotating shifts without night work, the share falls to 60%; for those in rotating shifts with night work or permanent night shifts, it is 65%. Workers in night and permanent night systems also report more deadline pressure, performance pressure, and overload due to workload. Occupational medicine guidelines on night and shift work also point to significantly elevated risks of heart attack, coronary events, and ischemic stroke. For overall mortality, the evidence is less clear; however, a widely cited long-term study of nurses found a 23% higher cardiovascular mortality risk after very long exposure to shift work. That is not enough for a clean statement such as "shift workers live X years less on average," but it is enough to conclude that long-term night and rotating shift work is not neutral from a health perspective.

There is a second problem that is often underestimated in the public debate: Germany has become better at keeping older workers in employment, but not necessarily at hiring them anew. The employment rate of those aged 60 to 64 rose from 53% to 67% between 2014 and 2024; among those aged 65 to 69, it rose from 14% to 21%. At first glance, this looks like a success story. But the IAB also shows that Germany has a high retention rate for older workers in existing jobs at 64.5%, while the hiring rate is comparatively low at just 5.8%; the OECD average here is 9.6%. In other words: older people who lose their jobs have significantly worse chances of getting back into employment. This is the labour-market Achilles' heel of any move toward retirement at 70.

The issue becomes even more acute when one looks at financial conditions in old age. The median net equivalised income of people aged 65 and over is currently €2,070 per month; for retired people aged 65 and over, it is €1,990. At the same time, one in five retired people has no more than €1,400 net available per month. The at-risk-of-poverty rate among those over 65 stood at 19.4% in 2024, and among women aged 65 and over it was 21.4%. These are no longer fringe figures; they point to a structural vulnerability in old age.

This vulnerability is especially significant in Germany because it is a country of renters. In 2024, 52.8% of the population lived in rented housing — the highest share in the EU. At the same time, 16% of all renter households had to spend more than 40% of their income on housing costs; among single-person renter households, the figure was as high as 24.7%. For many older people, the pension question is therefore also a housing-cost question. Those who do not live in fully paid-off owner-occupied housing in retirement, but must continue paying rent, depend much more heavily on a stable ongoing income.

Looking ahead over the next ten years also weakens simplistic reform arguments. According to the 2025 pension insurance report, the pension level will initially remain at 48% until 2031, but will then fall to 46.3% by 2039. At the same time, pensions are expected to rise by a nominal 45% by 2039, or an average of 2.8% per year. This means that pensions may be higher on paper, yet still become more strained relative to wages and living costs. This is a particularly critical outlook for renter households, whose largest expense item does not disappear in old age.

Against this backdrop, a blanket retirement age of 70 looks less like a neutral adjustment to demographic change and more like a social shift of risk. For well-paid people in relatively healthy, predictable occupations, a longer working life may be realistic. For people in physically demanding jobs, in shift or night work, with low incomes, and with poor chances of being rehired later in life, the calculation looks very different. In such cases, raising the age to 70 would often not mean three additional productive and well-paid years of work. More often, it would mean three extra years caught between exhaustion, precarious transitions, unemployment, or pension deductions.

That is why retirement at 70 is not merely a technical reform idea in Germany. It touches the question of how fairly a welfare state deals with unequal biographies. Anyone who simply compares average life expectancy with average retirement age overlooks the reality of a country in which manual workers, low earners, shift workers, and renters are far more vulnerable than other groups. A sustainable reform would therefore have to be more differentiated: with more flexible transitions, stronger protection for demanding occupations, better entry and re-entry chances for older workers, and a serious focus on housing costs in old age. Otherwise, retirement at 70 might create order on paper while producing new disorder in social reality.

Globalisation, EU Eastern Enlargement, and the Rise of the Far Right

Why economic change in Germany did not simply create prosperity, but also generated political explosive force

Anyone seeking to understand political developments in Germany and Europe should neither demonise nor romanticise globalisation and EU Eastern Enlargement. Both processes generated considerable economic dynamism, opened markets, made companies more competitive, and created major opportunities for Germany as an export nation. But they did not distribute their costs evenly. Since 1998, Germany's economic structure has shifted markedly: the share of workers in manufacturing fell from just over 31% to just over 23%, while services grew to around three-quarters of all workers. In parallel, the German middle class — according to the OECD — shrank from 70% to 64% of the population between 1995 and 2018. That is the backdrop against which many people now greet open markets and European integration not with a sense of progress, but with fear of downward mobility.

The decisive question is therefore not whether globalisation and Eastern Enlargement were "good" or "bad," but who had to bear their costs. Under the pressure of global competition, those most affected in Germany were above all workers in routine-heavy, tradeable occupations: industrial skilled workers, helpers, logistics and transport workers, people in simpler production roles, and workers with low qualifications and weak collective bargaining power. EU Eastern Enlargement did not simply double this development, but it did intensify it in certain segments. Research by the IAB and the Bundesbank shows that immigration from central and eastern Europe did not trigger broad displacement of native workers and that overall employment was actually supported. At the same time, however, it suppressed wage growth, above all in lower income groups. This was particularly visible in sectors where Eastern European workers and cross-border commuters are strongly represented: construction, logistics, transport, agriculture, metal and mechanical engineering, and simple helper activities. The result was not a general labour market collapse, but polarisation: more opportunity at the top, more competitive and cost pressure at the bottom.

The actual losers of both processes were therefore not "the Germans" as a whole, but certain social groups. These include, first, low- and lower-middle-skilled workers whose work is easily substitutable or internationally exposed to competition. Second, people in precarious forms of employment — agency workers, those on temporary contracts, and workers in subcontracting chains, who can barely buffer economic pressure. Third, regions that do not count among the winners of the new European division of labour, and where the loss of industrial stability was not compensated by new high-quality jobs. And fourth, a younger group of non-asset-owners who work but gain the impression that effort alone no longer translates into social advancement. It is precisely here that globalisation and Eastern Enlargement converge politically: both raised the pressure to adapt, but did not provide everyone with the same protective mechanisms.

This development became particularly explosive because it combined with the housing and ownership question. Neither globalisation nor EU Eastern Enlargement is the primary cause of high construction and property prices in Germany. The larger drivers are scarce building land, too little new construction, the concentration of good jobs in metropolitan areas, and years of cheap finance that further fuelled demand for property. The BBSR puts the need for new construction until 2030 at around 320,000 homes per year, while Destatis reports a price increase of 3.2% for new conventional residential buildings in November 2025 even compared to the previous year. EU Eastern Enlargement had an ambivalent effect here: it brought additional workers to shortage occupations, but simultaneously increased demand for housing in prospering regions. For price developments in housing, it is therefore more an amplifier than the underlying cause.

For ordinary earners, the political effect of these developments is enormous. Germany, with 52.8% renters, remains the EU's number-one renting nation, and 12.0% of the population in 2024 were considered overburdened by housing costs, having to spend more than 40% of their disposable income on housing. When home ownership is largely out of reach for large parts of the working population, economic insecurity is experienced not just as a wage problem, but as a blocked life trajectory. Those without an inheritance, a large deposit, or a position in the high-wage segments of the new economy experience the market not as a system of advancement, but as a mechanism of exclusion. That is the point at which the world of work, the housing market, and political alienation interconnect.

This is where the connection to the rise of extreme and radical right-wing parties begins. The Federal Agency for Civic Education (bpb) describes the classic "modernisation losers" thesis as a reaction to real or feared economic losses, social status decline, and the feeling that one's own group is losing influence and recognition. This mixture is clearly visible in Germany today: material insecurity, weakening promises of upward mobility, regional division, and the feeling that politics no longer represents the lived reality of many ordinary workers. The AfD received 20.8% of the secondary votes at the 2025 federal election. This does not mean its voters go to it exclusively for economic reasons. But economic vulnerability, housing stress, and status anxiety have created a milieu in which authoritarian, nationalist, and anti-immigration narratives land with particular force.

This pattern is also visible at the European level. The Friedrich Ebert Foundation notes, on the 2024 European elections, that the right-wing fringes of the party spectrum gained significantly. In the European Parliament, the groups Patriots for Europe, ECR, and ESN together hold 187 seats. The rise of extreme and radical right-wing parties is therefore not a purely German phenomenon, but the expression of a broader European development: where open markets, labour mobility, and social change are experienced as a loss of control — and where at the same time housing, security, and social recognition come under pressure — susceptibility grows to political forces promising national closure, cultural homogeneity, and a return to supposedly clear orders.

My conclusion is therefore: globalisation and EU Eastern Enlargement did not ruin Germany, but they did deepen a new social fault line. The winners were export-strong corporations, mobile high-skilled workers, urban centres, and asset-owners. The losers were above all those with little economic buffer: workers in routinised occupations, those in precarious employment, structurally weak regions, and younger non-owners. As long as politics does not credibly address this distributional question — through better wages in the lower and middle segment, more affordable housing, stronger regional development, and real opportunities for upward mobility without an inheritance — the social soil remains fertile for extreme parties to grow in. The political crisis is therefore not simply a crisis of migration. It runs deeper: it is a crisis of fairness, representation, and trust that open societies still offer their own citizens a reliable place.

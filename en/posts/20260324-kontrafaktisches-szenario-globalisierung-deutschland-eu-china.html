Counterfactual scenarios are not alternative truths, but a tool for understanding reality more clearly. The question here is therefore not whether China would have remained poor or insignificant without market-distorting interventions. That is not very plausible. Since the 1990s, China has genuinely and deeply integrated into the global economy. According to World Bank/WITS, trade as a share of GDP rose from 24.23% in 1990 to 63.57% in 2006. At the same time, tariffs fell sharply: the simple average tariff was still 39.71% in 1992, while the weighted average tariff stood at 32.17%; in the current WITS data for 2023, the weighted average is 2.17%. So China was by no means a closed economy.

The decisive point is another one: openness is not the same thing as reciprocity. From the perspective of many trading partners, China opened its markets selectively, while the state retained an exceptionally strong role in strategic sectors. In its 2024 trade policy review, the WTO noted that many members still had serious doubts about the quality and completeness of China's subsidy notifications. Many members also viewed China's subsidy practices as a driver of market distortions and excess capacity. The OECD reaches a similarly clear conclusion: firms based in China received more state support relative to their revenues than firms in other surveyed economies; particularly striking was the rise in advantages from below-market financing, from around USD 22 billion in 2020 to more than USD 46 billion in 2023.

Exchange rates also suggest that competitive conditions were, for a considerable period, not purely market-driven. The IMF described the renminbi in 2012 as "moderately undervalued"; by 2015, it concluded that the renminbi was no longer undervalued. This matters for the counterfactual scenario: it suggests that there really was a meaningful period of additional price competitiveness, but that it was time-limited. A more reciprocal China would therefore not have meant that the country's rise failed to happen. It would more likely have meant that this rise was less export-driven, less price-aggressive, and probably more supported by domestic demand.

How might China itself have developed under such a scenario? Probably more slowly, but more healthily. In reality, China's current account surplus reached roughly 10% of GDP in 2007; in its latest China report, the IMF again describes how strong exports compensate in part for weak private domestic demand, and how tendencies toward real RMB depreciation due to lower inflation in China can intensify external imbalances. A more reciprocal model would probably have pushed China earlier toward consumption, private demand, and a more productive allocation of capital. Growth would likely have been less spectacular, but also less vulnerable to excess capacity, credit-driven expansion, and external tensions. That is not a certainty, but it is the most plausible inference from the available IMF and OECD findings.

For Germany, the picture would likely have been mixed, but on balance somewhat more favorable for industrial stability. One important nuance matters here: Germany differed for a long time from other Western economies. A major study of the German labor market for the period 1988 to 2008 shows that the rise of the East as a whole supported or preserved around 442,000 additional full-time jobs in Germany. However, the authors stress explicitly that this positive overall effect was driven almost entirely by Eastern Europe, not by China. For China alone, the negative import effect in Germany was present, but much smaller than often claimed, while the positive export effect remained statistically imprecise. In a scenario of more reciprocal Chinese behavior, Germany therefore would probably not simply have "won," but it likely would have experienced fewer local stresses in import-competing industries and would have entered later competition in sectors such as machinery, intermediate goods, or certain technology fields from a somewhat more stable base.

For the EU as a whole, the counterfactual balance looks clearer. The ECB estimates that an increase in imports from China of €1,000 per worker in a sector between 2015 and 2022 was associated with a decline in that sector's employment rate of 0.1 percentage points. Extrapolated across the euro area, this corresponds to around 240,000 jobs that disappeared or were shifted into less exposed sectors. In a scenario without the added force of strategic subsidies and earlier exchange-rate undervaluation, this adjustment pressure would probably have been weaker. The EU would therefore likely have retained more industrial employment, more regional stability, and somewhat less political adjustment pressure.

However, this alternative scenario would not have been free of cost. Europe would very likely have paid somewhat higher prices. In 2024, China was the EU's largest import partner: the Union imported goods worth €517.8 billion from China and exported €213.3 billion to it; the trade deficit stood at €304.5 billion. China thus accounted for 21.3% of all extra-EU imports. The ECB has also shown that developments in China have a noticeable impact on import prices in the euro area. A less price-aggressive China would therefore probably have provided less relief to European consumers and many import-dependent firms. The counterpart to that would have been a more robust employment structure. Put differently: globalization would likely have been less cheap, but also less fragile for Europe.

The difference would probably have been especially visible in strategic industries. The European Commission now states openly that the EU is almost completely dependent on imports from China for solar PV modules. In its latest report, it gives striking figures for EU supply: 94% of photovoltaic modules and cells, 79% of wafers, and 50% of inverters come from China; for batteries, around 79% of EU imports in 2024 came from China. In a more reciprocal scenario, this concentration would probably have been weaker. Europe would not automatically have become the global leader, but it likely would have retained more market share, more time to scale up, and fewer strategic dependencies.

The real conclusion of this thought experiment is therefore not a simple zero-sum story. China would probably not have declined, but rather grown somewhat more slowly and in a more balanced way. Germany and the EU, in turn, would likely have experienced fewer harsh displacement shocks, more industrial substance, and lower dependencies. In exchange, the price advantages of cheap Chinese imports would have been smaller. Globalization would then probably not have supplied Europe with inexpensive goods to the same degree as the actual historical path. But it would likely have been somewhat better balanced socially and industrially.

Lately I keep hearing people discuss whether an authoritarian democracy(!) might not be better than a liberal, constitutional democracy, and urgently necessary if we want to stand up to China. What a foolish debate!

Liberal democracy is not fast, not simple, not comfortable, and often not efficient.
But what it does better than any other form of government human beings have devised is this:
It protects ordinary citizens from the arbitrariness of the powerful.

The inventors of democracy were brilliant! They understood human nature exactly!

The central term itself is already imprecise. In political science, an "authoritarian democracy" is not a clear or stable category. Freedom House distinguishes between electoral democracies and liberal democracies on the basis of political rights and civil liberties, and V-Dem systematically distinguishes between liberal democracies, electoral democracies, electoral autocracies, and closed autocracies. A state can therefore hold elections and still be authoritarian if courts are weak, the media are unfree, and power is not effectively constrained.

Anyone who looks only at speed can easily draw the wrong conclusions. Authoritarian systems often do have a real advantage in speed and procedural simplicity: fewer veto players, fewer public arenas of conflict, fewer institutional blockages. Decisions can be imposed more centrally and often more quickly. But this concentration of power is also the source of their greatest problem: errors become easier to magnify, criticism becomes easier to suppress, and corrections often come too late. Recent research on authoritarian knowledge and advisory systems describes a genuine vicious circle: expertise is adapted to the interests of power, independent advice is weakened, and political misjudgment becomes more likely.

That is why "efficiency" has to be defined carefully. In a narrow sense, an authoritarian regime may be procedurally more efficient because it decides faster. In the more demanding sense — measured by reliability, capacity to learn, ability to correct mistakes, and long-term performance — much speaks in favor of liberal constitutional democracy. Precisely because it is slow, because it allows dissent, because courts, parliaments, media, and opposition parties are allowed to obstruct, it can expose and correct bad decisions more easily. What looks like friction in the short run often turns out to be institutional self-correction in the long run.

When it comes to freedom and human rights, the comparison becomes much clearer. Freedom House measures political rights and civil liberties worldwide and explicitly states in its methodology that freedom is best realized in liberal democratic orders. V-Dem also shows for 2026 that 74 percent of the world's population now lives in autocracies, only 7 percent in liberal democracies, and that freedom of expression is the most heavily attacked component of democratic order. The report also notes that repression, including torture, is increasingly used in more countries to eliminate political opponents.

The difference becomes even clearer when it comes to the rule of law. The World Justice Project Rule of Law Index measures, among other things, constraints on government power, control of corruption, open government, fundamental rights, and the functioning of civil and criminal justice. At the top of the global rankings in the 2024 index are Denmark, Norway, Finland, Sweden, and Germany — all liberal, constitutional democracies. This does not prove that every democracy automatically functions well. But it shows very clearly in which type of regime strong rule-of-law guarantees are most often found empirically.

Economically, the picture is more differentiated, but in the end it also favors liberal democracy. Yes, there are authoritarian states that have gone through impressive phases of growth. But serious long-term research does not say that authoritarianism is superior on average. A widely cited NBER study by Acemoglu, Naidu, Restrepo, and Robinson concludes that democratization can raise GDP per capita by about 20 percent in the long run, through channels such as higher investment, more education, economic reform, better public goods, and less social unrest. On this view, democracy is not a luxury one can afford after growth; it is often itself part of the foundation of growth.

Even more important is the question of how durable economic success really is. An IMF working paper from 2023 finds that growth under democracies is more likely to be sustainable, that output collapses under autocracies last longer, and that stagnation under autocracy is more likely to turn into outright collapse. A more recent NBER paper on types of autocracy adds that personalist autocracies in particular are marked by lower average growth rates and much greater volatility. Autocracies can produce spectacular success stories, but as a group they are more volatile, more crisis-prone, and less reliable.

There is also a problem that is regularly underestimated in popular debates: the credibility of the numbers themselves. A World Bank study comparing official economic growth with night-light data finds that, for the same increase in night-time light intensity, more authoritarian regimes report higher official GDP growth. This is interpreted as evidence of stronger manipulation of official statistics in autocracies. Anyone claiming that authoritarian systems are generally more economically successful must therefore also explain why it is precisely there that the independent institutions needed to verify such success claims are often missing.

That leaves the authoritarian promise with relatively little: more speed, more capacity to impose decisions, more visible determination. But that is politically dangerous precisely when power is not effectively constrained. Liberal constitutional democracy is slower, more laborious, and often frustrating. It produces conflict, delay, and compromise. Yet that is exactly where its strength lies. It protects freedom not through the good intentions of rulers, but through institutions that limit rule itself. It does not make errors impossible, but it makes them more visible.

That is why the real answer to the longing for a supposedly "more efficient" authoritarian state is fairly simple: anyone who takes freedom, human rights, the rule of law, and a robust long-term prosperity order seriously should not let themselves be dazzled by the speed of authoritarian systems. The liberal element of democracy is not decoration. It is its safety device against the very human nature your quote refers to: vanity, lust for power, self-deception, and the constant temptation of the powerful to regard themselves as infallible. That is precisely why liberal constitutional democracy is, in the long run, the stronger form of government.

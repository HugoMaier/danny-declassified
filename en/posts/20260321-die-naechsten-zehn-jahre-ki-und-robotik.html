Ten years ago, modern generative AI barely existed in public life, and most advanced robots were still expensive, brittle machines for tightly controlled settings. Today, frontier models are being optimized for reasoning, tool use, computer control, and longer-running agentic work, while robotics has moved from hype videos into early factory and warehouse deployments. If that arc continues, the 2030s will probably not bring either a clean utopia or a robot apocalypse. More likely, they will bring a world in which intelligence becomes abundant in software, physical automation spreads unevenly, and societies fight over who captures the gains.

What makes this worth taking seriously is the speed of the recent shift. Stanford's 2025 AI Index says performance on difficult benchmarks jumped sharply in a single year, and the inference cost for systems performing at GPT‑3.5 level fell more than 280-fold between November 2022 and October 2024. On the robotics side, the International Federation of Robotics reports that the global stock of industrial robots reached about 4.28 million in 2023 and has more than doubled over the last decade, with annual installations above half a million units for three consecutive years. That combination matters: cheaper intelligence plus steadily rising automation usually means broader diffusion, not stagnation.

The current frontier also points in a specific direction. OpenAI describes GPT‑5.4 as bringing together reasoning, coding, and agentic workflows, while Anthropic says Claude Opus 4.6 and Sonnet 4.6 are designed for stronger coding, longer agentic tasks, and very large context windows. Google has been explicit that Gemini is being developed for an "agentic era," with Project Astra, Project Mariner, and Gemini Robotics extending model capabilities from chat into action in browsers and, increasingly, the physical world.

Robotics is also no longer just a research story. BMW says humanoid robots from Figure were deployed at its Spartanburg plant in 2025 and supported production of more than 30,000 BMW X3 vehicles over roughly ten months. Agility Robotics describes Digit as the first humanoid robot in production deployment and has announced commercial agreements with customers including Mercado Libre and Toyota Motor Manufacturing Canada. Hyundai has said it plans to begin deploying the production version of Boston Dynamics' Atlas at its Georgia plant starting in 2028, while Reuters reported this week that Skild AI and Nvidia are already putting generalized robot-control software onto Foxconn assembly lines producing Blackwell servers in Houston.

So what happens if we project the broad direction of the last ten years into the next ten? Not linearly, and not naively. Technologies usually improve faster than institutions, laws, labor markets, and social norms. That is why the most plausible futures are mixed ones: everyday life changes dramatically, but not all at once; work is reorganized more than fully erased; and geopolitics becomes more tense because AI increasingly depends on chips, cloud infrastructure, and energy. The IEA projects global data-centre electricity consumption to roughly double by 2030 in its base case, which is a reminder that the future of AI is also a future of grids, power, land, and industrial policy.

Scenario 1: The Coordinated AI Decade

In the more optimistic version, the 2030s become a decade of managed abundance. By 2036, most people in wealthy countries — and a growing share elsewhere — have a persistent AI agent tied to their devices, accounts, and workflows. It handles scheduling, email triage, travel booking, procurement, basic tax preparation, bureaucratic paperwork, first-draft writing, tutoring, translation, and personal knowledge management. This does not require science fiction. It is a direct extension of today's frontier model roadmaps: longer context, better memory, better tool use, better computer control, and more reliable multi-step execution.

Everyday life in this world is calmer, faster, and more customized. Schools still have teachers, but every student also has a tutor. Doctors still matter, but patients arrive better prepared because their AI has already summarized symptoms, insurance constraints, medication histories, and the latest treatment guidance. Government services become less humiliating because forms, claims, and appeals are navigated with machine assistance rather than by exhausted citizens alone. In this future, the real social revolution is not a humanoid servant in every hallway; it is the disappearance of a huge amount of low-grade friction. That is the most plausible consumer impact because software scales much faster than physical hardware.

The job market in this optimistic scenario changes profoundly but does not implode. The World Economic Forum's Future of Jobs Report 2025 found that employers already expect AI and information processing and robotics to be major business transformers by 2030, and that 39% of workers' core skills are expected to change by then. In a coordinated version of the 2030s, governments and firms take that warning seriously. They build retraining systems early, redesign occupations around human judgment, and shift education toward communication, domain expertise, systems thinking, and supervision of AI tools. White-collar jobs become more productive and more compressed, but many survive in altered form because trust, accountability, and exception-handling remain stubbornly human.

Physical work also evolves rather than vanishing. Robots spread first where the economics are clearest: warehouses, fulfillment centers, semiconductor facilities, large factories, eldercare assistance, hospitals, and dangerous industrial tasks. Humans remain central in mixed environments that require dexterity, empathy, improvisation, and liability-bearing decisions. The key shift is that many jobs stop being purely manual or purely cognitive and become orchestration roles inside human-machine systems. Someone who once moved boxes may supervise fleets, intervene on exceptions, maintain robotic systems, and manage throughput data. Someone who once drafted reports may supervise five agents, audit outputs, and handle stakeholder negotiation.

Geopolitically, this optimistic scenario is still competitive, but stable enough to remain governable. The United States, Europe, China, and a few allied technology blocs compete fiercely over chips, cloud capacity, and industrial robotics, yet they also converge on partial rules around model deployment, safety testing, and infrastructure resilience. AI becomes a strategic industry like energy, telecoms, or aerospace, but not one that destroys the basic possibility of cooperation. In that world, the countries that do best are not simply the ones with the biggest models; they are the ones that can pair compute, energy, education, and industrial upgrading into a coherent national strategy.

The social mood in this future is surprisingly decent. People complain about AI, fear it, and resent some of its labor-market effects, but they also benefit from it every day. Productivity rises enough to support shorter turnaround times, better health-system navigation, more accessible education, and some reduction in administrative burden. The political tension does not disappear, but it is softened by visible gains. By 2036, AI is treated less like magic and more like electricity: powerful, often invisible, and embedded in nearly everything. That strikes me as the most hopeful plausible scenario.

Scenario 2: The Uneven Machine Age

The darker scenario starts from the same technical facts but assumes weaker adaptation. Models keep getting cheaper, faster, and more agentic; robotics keeps improving in structured settings; and businesses race ahead of institutions. In this world, the technology works well enough to transform work, but politics does not move fast enough to cushion the shock. Everyday life still becomes AI-saturated, but the benefits are distributed unevenly. Affluent professionals get excellent agents, premium tools, and high-quality personalized services. Everyone else gets thinner, cheaper AI systems that help, but also surveil, nudge, and deskill.

In this version of 2036, the labor market becomes harsher than many expected. Routine cognitive work is hit first: support roles, junior analysis, document production, scheduling, coordination, compliance prep, and large parts of coding and media production. The OECD has already been warning that rapid technological progress, falling costs, and growing AI-skill availability could put economies on the cusp of an AI revolution. In the darker scenario, that revolution arrives before institutions are ready. The result is not mass total unemployment, but a painful hollowing-out of entry-level ladders and middle-skill office work. It becomes harder to "start small and learn on the job" because the starter tasks are precisely the ones the machines now do best.

Robotics deepens the divide. Humanoid and semi-humanoid systems do not take over every café, home, or street. But they become good enough for a growing band of repetitive, semi-structured physical tasks, especially in logistics, assembly, inspection, handling, and industrial support. That weakens the old idea that displaced white-collar workers can simply "move into physical jobs," because those jobs are also being automated from below. The labor market does not disappear; it polarizes. High-autonomy, high-trust, and high-social-skill work retains value. So does elite technical work. Everything else feels more precarious.

The political economy of this darker future is more combustible. A few companies and states capture an outsized share of the gains because frontier AI depends on concentrated assets: advanced chips, giant data centers, scarce engineering talent, proprietary data, and reliable power. The IEA's projections on data-centre electricity demand suggest that AI capacity itself will depend heavily on energy build-out. That means AI competition increasingly overlaps with energy competition, land use, permitting, and state subsidies. The winners are not just good at software. They are good at hard infrastructure.

Geopolitics, accordingly, hardens. Recent Reuters reporting shows how entangled AI has already become with export controls, defense procurement, and strategic rivalry: OpenAI is reportedly expanding sales to U.S. government agencies through AWS for classified and unclassified work; Reuters also reported official U.S. claims that DeepSeek trained a model on Nvidia Blackwell chips despite export restrictions; and Washington is weighing new chip-export rules tied to foreign investments and security conditions. In the darker scenario, these are not temporary disputes but the early shape of a more fragmented order in which AI capability is treated as military-industrial capacity.

Daily life under this scenario is more efficient, but less free-feeling. People rely on AI for almost everything, yet trust it less. Work becomes more measured, more monitored, and more contingent because AI makes it easier to quantify output and compare workers directly against automated alternatives. Social conflict intensifies because people can see abundance at the top while feeling replaceable below. In that world, the central political argument of the 2030s is not whether AI is useful. It is who it is for.

What I think is most plausible

My own best guess is that 2036 lands between these two futures, but closer to the optimistic one in daily life and closer to the darker one in politics. Software agents will likely spread faster than robots because they are cheaper to deploy and easier to improve. Robotics will keep advancing, but mainly in structured, high-ROI environments first. So the headline change by 2036 will probably be this: most knowledge work will be done with agents by default, while a meaningful but narrower slice of physical work will be reshaped by robots.

That means the biggest question is not whether AI will matter. It will. The bigger question is whether societies can build the institutions to absorb the shock: education systems that teach people to work with agents, labor policies that preserve mobility, competition policy that prevents extreme concentration, and industrial strategies that link compute to energy and broad-based productivity rather than to narrow rent extraction alone. The technical trajectory looks strong. The social trajectory is still undecided.

And that, in the end, is why the next ten years will not just be a story about AI models or robots. They will be a story about power: power over work, over infrastructure, over states, and over the tempo of everyday life. The machines are getting better very quickly. The real question is whether our politics can keep up.

Globalisation, the Labour Market, and Home Ownership in Germany

Essay draft for Danny Declassified

18 March 2026

The Labour Market and Globalisation

Looking at Germany since 1998, what becomes clear is this: globalisation has not simply made the country "poorer" overall, but it has distributed gains broadly while concentrating losses sharply. The overall structure of the labour market has shifted markedly from manufacturing to services: the share of employees in manufacturing fell from 29.8% in 1998 to 22.8% in 2025, while services rose from 68.2% to 75.9%. At the same time, the Institute for Employment Research (IAB) shows that, across Germany as a whole, import competition and export opportunities largely balanced out during the 2000s — but not for all sectors and regions: in import-exposed industries, employment tenures shortened, while export-strong sectors benefited.

In my assessment, the groups hardest hit were primarily middle-income industrial and skilled workers in routine-heavy, tradeable occupations — people in segments such as clothing, consumer electronics, electronic components, furniture, and historically sectors like footwear. The IAB describes very concretely that workers at, for example, manufacturers of electronic components suffered job losses more often, while workers in automotive and mechanical engineering held more stable positions. The OECD summarises international research on Germany as follows: offshoring particularly depressed employment and wages among workers with mid-level qualifications performing routine tasks. An additional German factor was that temporary or agency workers in strongly internationally integrated sectors bore a higher risk of unemployment. And those who slid from a mid-level occupation into a low-skilled job suffered, in median terms, actual nominal wage losses in Germany from the mid-1990s onwards. Regionally, this hit particularly specialised areas hard; the IAB explicitly names the Südwestpfalz as an example of an entire regional collapse following the decline of the footwear industry.

It is important to note: the deterioration of many working conditions was not caused solely by globalisation. But it coincided with the same era as greater precarity and polarisation. Official Destatis figures show that the number of atypically employed workers among the core working-age population rose from 5.426 million in 1998 to 6.408 million in 2024; in the 2000s it was even higher. The OECD also notes that the share of low-wage workers in Germany increased sharply from the mid-1990s; by 2018, the number of low-wage employees had grown by 60%. My point is therefore: it was not "the Germans" as a whole who suffered equally from globalisation, but above all those who had neither export-strong winning sectors, high mobility, academic qualifications, nor assets to fall back on.

Construction Costs, Ownership, and Affordability

In housing, the connection is similar: globalisation is a factor, but not the primary cause of high construction and living costs. Its direct influence ran mainly through global supply chains, commodity prices, and energy prices. Destatis shows how powerfully this hit in recent years: structural timber prices rose by 77.3% on an annual average in 2021, rebar steel rose by 40.4% in 2022, and in the first half of 2023 cement was still 41.7% above the previous year's level. Construction prices followed suit; in November 2022, prices for new conventional residential buildings were 16.9% above the prior year, and even in November 2025 they were still 3.2% higher. This means: global interconnectedness clearly drove up construction costs in crisis years. But it does not alone explain why home ownership has become so difficult for average earners.

For the ownership problem, other drivers are more important in the long run: a shortage of building land, the spatial concentration of good jobs in metropolitan areas, regulation and standards, financing costs, and investor demand. The BBSR shows that median land prices for individual construction rose by 27% across Germany between 2011 and 2016, and by 41.5% in expensive major cities. Destatis notes that the house price index stood at 148.0 in 2024 despite the correction following the interest rate turnaround — roughly 48% above 2015 levels; the building land price index had already reached 187.5 in 2021. The OECD also describes two mechanisms directly linked to globalisation and financial market liberalisation: first, the geographic polarisation of jobs drives prices up in urban areas; second, low interest rates and tax-advantaged real estate investment have drawn institutional and private investors into the German property market. According to the OECD, this has intensified the concentration of wealth in real estate and pushed many middle-class households out of the owner-occupied housing market.

The formula "ordinary earners can barely afford properties any more" is therefore very accurate for many large cities and their surrounding areas, but somewhat too sweeping for Germany as a whole. In 2024, Germany — with 52.8% renters — remains the EU's number-one renting nation. In OECD data, Germany is one of the few countries where renter households outnumber owner households; private renting at 52% is the most common form of tenure. Particularly telling is the distribution by income: in the lowest income quintile, only 23% of households in Germany live in owner-occupied housing. At the same time, 12.0% of the population in 2024 were overburdened by housing costs, meaning they had to spend more than 40% of their disposable income on housing. For young and asset-poor households, the threshold for ownership is therefore high, even if affordability improved slightly in 2024/25 as inflation fell and interest rates eased somewhat.

Social Consequences

In my view, the social consequences are profound. First, wealth accumulation is becoming increasingly unequal: those who already own property or inherited capital benefit from rising asset values; those who rent find it harder to save. The OECD shows that housing now consumes roughly a third of disposable income for the German middle class, compared to a quarter in the mid-1990s. Second, the spatial divide is growing between prospering centres and their commuter zones on one side and left-behind regions on the other; the OECD emphasises that rising house prices in large urban areas have intensified regional inequality. Third, a feeling is forming that effort alone is no longer enough: someone who earns a decent wage but cannot build up property without an inheritance, and cannot easily move to winning regions, experiences the market not as a system of advancement but as a mechanism of exclusion. The OECD also shows that lower incomes correlate regionally with greater dissatisfaction with economic conditions and institutions. The political conclusion I draw is therefore: globalisation did not become a problem in Germany primarily because it was "wrong," but because its costs remained stuck with particular occupational groups, regions, and younger non-property-owners, while labour incomes and opportunities for ownership diverged.

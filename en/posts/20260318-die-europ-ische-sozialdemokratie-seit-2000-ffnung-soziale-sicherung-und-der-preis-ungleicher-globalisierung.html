European Social Democracy Since 2000

Openness, Social Security, and the Price of Unequal Globalisation

Why social democracy in Germany and Europe did not simply fail "because of globalisation," but because of the political question of how openness can be translated into social security.

Anyone seeking to understand the development of European social democracy since 2000 should fall into neither the simple narrative of "betrayal of the working class" nor the counter-legend of mere culture war. The real conflict lay deeper: social democratic parties wanted to be simultaneously the agents of modernisation, European integration, and global openness — while also remaining the political force guaranteeing social protection, upward mobility, and security. It is precisely this mediation that has become fractured in many places. I have already analysed in depth the economic side of this development in my research on globalisation, EU Eastern Enlargement, and their social and political consequences.

In Germany, this shift is particularly clear in the SPD. At the 2002 federal election it still achieved 38.5% of secondary votes. What followed was 34.2% in 2005, 23.0% in 2009, 25.7% in 2013, 20.5% in 2017, a temporary recovery with 25.7% in 2021, and finally 16.4% in 2025. At EU level, the development was similar: the social democratic party family held 191 seats in the European Parliament in 2014, 154 seats in the constitutive parliament in 2019, 138 seats in the outgoing parliament of 2019–2024, and 136 seats after the 2024 European elections. Social democracy has thus not disappeared, but has moved from a hegemony-capable mainstream party family to a significantly smaller, more defensive force.

Programmatically, social democracy was for a long time by no means anti-globalist. On the contrary: the classic line was that there was no way back to the old national industrial society, and that the real task was to shape globalisation democratically. The SPD's Hamburg Programme formulates this explicitly; it also states that social Europe must be the answer to globalisation. At the European level too, the line was similar: social democratic actors linked economic integration to the expectation that it had to be socially flanked and democratically framed. The original course was therefore not closure, but regulated openness.

The problem began where this regulated openness no longer appeared in the social reality of many voters as protection, but as a loss of control. This is precisely where the economic findings are decisive. In my research on globalisation, I argue that Germany did not simply "lose" overall, but that gains were distributed broadly while losses were concentrated: export-strong sectors benefited, while import-exposed, routine-heavy, and regionally clustered labour markets came under greater pressure. This diagnosis fits well with more recent research showing that regional import shocks from low-wage countries could measurably weaken support for social democratic parties in Europe.

The picture of EU Eastern Enlargement is similarly differentiated. It was not a blanket job-destroyer for Germany. The findings I have assembled in my research show: there was no simple mass displacement of native workers, but there were segmented pressure effects on wages, bargaining power, and the perception of competition in lower and lower-middle labour market segments. This distinction is politically important. When a party like social democracy correctly points out at the macroeconomic level that integration creates prosperity, while locally wage restraint, housing pressure, or status insecurity increase noticeably, a credibility problem arises. The perception of Europe and globalisation then tips from "opportunity" to "burden" — even when the overall economic balance remains positive.

Added to this was that this development in Germany coincided with an erosion of the classic middle. According to the OECD, the German middle-income group shrank from 70% to 64% of the population between 1995 and 2018; a large part of this decline fell in the early 2000s. During exactly this phase, everyday experience shifted for many people: more employment did not automatically mean more security, and open markets did not automatically mean more upward mobility. In my research on the housing market, globalisation, and political shifts, the same mechanism keeps appearing: not abstract openness itself, but its unequally distributed everyday consequences — insecurity, status pressure, a shortage of housing, and regional disconnection — became politically effective.

The refugee crisis of 2015/16 worked less as a sole cause in this context than as an accelerant. Where economic insecurity, integration stress, a housing shortage, and cultural conflicts already existed, migration policy could be read as a cipher for a general loss of control. Social democracy then came under pressure from two sides: from the left, because it had not corrected social hardships sufficiently, and from the right, because it could be attacked as the party of open Europe, free movement, and liberal democracy. The consequence was not only a loss of votes to left-wing parties or the Greens, but also the migration of traditional working-class and skilled-worker milieus to the right.

In the 2020s, European social democracy is responding to this with a markedly changed language. In the PES manifesto for 2024, it is no longer the optimistic globalisation narrative of the early 2000s that sets the tone, but a line of regulated openness: European strategic autonomy, reindustrialisation, a "Made in Europe" approach, stronger social standards, better workers' rights, and a more active industrial and investment policy. This is not a repudiation of Europe or the world economy, but a visible correction of the earlier course. Social democracy today is trying again more strongly to bind openness to protection, industrial policy, and social sovereignty.

This also yields the actual conclusion. European social democratic parties have not been weakened since 2000 because they were "too European" or "too international." They became weaker where they could no longer credibly translate the distributional consequences of globalisation, EU integration, and Eastern Enlargement into politics. The historic strength of social democracy always lay in not stopping economic change, but in embedding it socially. Its problem since the 2000s has been that it could no longer convincingly guarantee exactly this embedding in many places. The crisis was therefore less a crisis of openness than a crisis of social mediation. And that is precisely why its future will not be decided by the question of whether it is for or against globalisation, but by whether it can again turn openness into concretely experienced security.

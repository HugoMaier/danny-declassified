A Fictional War Scenario: What a Major Conflict with China, Russia, and Iran Would Mean for Germany and the EU

An analysis of the probable consequences for supply, the economy, and societal stability

Essay

An important note first: the following scenario is an analytical exercise, not a forecast. The purpose is not to "play through" a war, but to understand the real dependencies that would make Germany and the EU vulnerable in an extreme geopolitical crisis. The core of that vulnerability is now officially acknowledged: the European Commission identifies supply chains, critical infrastructure, technology security, and the political "weaponisation" of economic dependencies as central risks to European economic security. If several of these risks materialise simultaneously, a trade disruption would rapidly become a comprehensive supply, economic, and confidence crisis. [Sources: EC Economic Security 2024]

For Germany, the first shock would very likely be a massive collapse in intermediate products, electronics, and industrial goods from China and East Asia. China was Germany's most important trading partner in 2025 again, with a volume of 251.8 billion euros. For the EU as a whole, China was the third-largest export partner and the most important import partner in 2024; the EU exported goods worth 213.3 billion euros to China and imported goods worth 517.8 billion euros. In a war that severely disrupts trade with or through East Asia, it would therefore not only be consumption that would be affected, but above all Germany's industrial functionality and that of the single market. [Sources: Destatis 2026; Eurostat 2025]

The second shock would very likely come via sea and energy routes. Around 20% of global container traffic and approximately 40% of trade between Asia and Europe flows through the Red Sea and the Suez Canal. At the same time, around 20 million barrels per day pass through the Strait of Hormuz — roughly a quarter of global seaborne oil trade — as well as approximately 19% of global LNG trade. Even brief interruptions to these routes would have significant market consequences, according to the IEA. For Europe this would mean: significantly higher freight rates, longer transport times, price spikes in energy and intermediate goods, and rapidly rising burdens for chemicals, transport, agriculture, electricity prices, and consumer budgets. [Sources: EP Red Sea 2024; IEA 2026]

For the supply situation, it would be particularly critical that several bottleneck sectors came under pressure simultaneously. For medicines, supply chains are long, complex, and heavily dependent on Asia according to the European Court of Auditors; the report references estimates that the EU depends on Asia for around 70% of active pharmaceutical ingredients and 79% of pharmaceutical precursors. For semiconductors, the starting position is similarly strained: the European Court of Auditors emphasises that the Chips Act was created precisely in response to shortages and the strong dependence on non-European supply chains. For critical raw materials, the EU has itself formulated in the Critical Raw Materials Act the goal of not sourcing more than 65% of a strategic raw material from a single third country by 2030; the ECB points out that China currently supplies around 70% of the eurozone's rare earths. In a major war, shortages in medicines, electronics, industrial spare parts, magnets, battery materials, and defence components would therefore be very plausible. [Sources: ECA 2025 medicines; ECA 2025 chips; CRMA; ECB 2025]

In the energy sector in particular, the situation in Germany and the EU would tighten. Natural gas still covers around 20% of total EU energy consumption. In the third quarter of 2025, 59.9% of EU LNG imports came from the US; for pipeline gas, Norway was the most important supplier at 51.8%. This means: even after the turn away from Russia, Europe remains dependent on external energy anchors. If in a fictional major war Middle Eastern routes were simultaneously disrupted, LNG markets strained, and the US unavailable or otherwise engaged as a normal economic and security-policy stabiliser, Europe would with high probability again face an energy price shock. For Germany as a large industrial economy, this would hit chemicals, metals, glass, paper, logistics, and energy-intensive production particularly hard. [Source: Eurostat 2025 energy imports]

Economically, this would for Germany probably be more than an ordinary recession. The shock would simultaneously hit supply, prices, production, trade, and expectations. Many companies would not simply be able to switch to alternative suppliers, because critical components or intermediate products are only processed in a few countries. This would initially lead to production stoppages, short-time working, prioritisation of critical goods, and postponed investment. For the EU, the single market would be a major buffer, because it allows joint procurement, rerouting of goods flows, and coordinated crisis measures. But even at EU level, the economy would likely move towards stronger state direction: strategic reserves, emergency procurement, prioritised allocation, and a kind of "war economy light" for particularly critical areas such as health, energy, semiconductors, raw materials, and transport. This conclusion is derived from documented dependencies and the already existing EU instruments for economic security. [Sources: EC Economic Security; Critical Medicines Act]

Socially, the effect would probably be at least as grave as the economic one. A simultaneous shock to prices, medicines, energy, delivery times, and digital infrastructure would shake the sense of security of many people deeply. Added to this is the fact that maritime disruptions in the Red Sea can affect not just goods traffic but also digital connectivity: the European Parliament noted in 2024 that damaged undersea cables in the region affect around 25% of data traffic between Asia and Europe. In Germany and the EU, a mixture of scarcity fears, distributional conflicts, political scapegoating, and growing susceptibility to disinformation would therefore be expected. The real danger would not only be shortage, but a shock to trust: the impression that the state, the market, and European integration can no longer deliver reliably in an extreme crisis. [Sources: EP Red Sea 2024]

My overall assessment is therefore: in such a fictional war scenario, Germany and the EU would very probably not "collapse," but they would enter a sharp phase of scarcity, price increases, industrial disruption, and societal polarisation. Europe's greatest strengths would be the single market, institutional coordination capacity, and the still-existing industrial base. Its greatest weaknesses would be the high external dependence on energy, critical intermediate goods, medicines, semiconductors, and raw materials, as well as the political vulnerability of open societies under stress. That is precisely why today's debate on resilience is more than economic policy: at its core it is a debate about crisis-resistance, state capacity to act, and democratic stability. [Sources: EC Economic Security; CRMA; Critical Medicines Act]

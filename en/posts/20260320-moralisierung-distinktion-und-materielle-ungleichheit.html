Moralisation, Distinction, and Material Inequality

On the political culture of privileged milieus in Germany

Teaser: This piece develops the hypothesis that the moralisation frequently observed in public debates among privileged milieus in Germany can partly be understood as a symbolic processing of material inequality. The argument is deliberately cautiously formulated: not psychological certainty, but a social-scientific interpretation of value change, distinction, status logics, and unequal living conditions stands at its centre.

Introduction

In what follows, the expression "upper class" is not used in the narrow sense of a small wealth elite, but as a heuristic label for economically secure, highly educated, and culturally influential upper milieus. The guiding question is whether the moralisation frequently observed in public debates among such milieus can partly be interpreted as a compensation mechanism — that is, as a symbolic processing of the fact that one's own living situation is materially far more favourable than that of many low-wage earners and groups at risk of poverty. This thesis is not meant as psychological certainty, but as a social-scientific hypothesis. It presupposes that moral language does not only express convictions, but also fulfils social functions.

Material Starting Point

The starting point is a society that is simultaneously prosperous and unequal. According to the Bundesbank study on the economic situation of private households, the wealthiest ten percent of households in Germany held 54% of total net wealth in 2023. Average net wealth stood at around 324,800 euros, while the median was only 76,000 euros. In parallel, according to Destatis, around 17.6 million people, or 20.9% of the population, were at risk of poverty or social exclusion in 2024. In the low-wage sector, 16% of employees worked in 2023; the official low-wage threshold was 13.04 euros gross per hour. These data point to a considerable distance between secure and precarious living situations.

Value Change and Post-materialist Orientations

A first explanation for morally highly charged discourses among privileged milieus lies in value change. Political culture research has long described how, with growing material security, post-materialist orientations gain in weight. Questions of self-realisation, recognition, environment, cultural sensitivity, and lifestyle then move more strongly to the foreground. The Federal Agency for Civic Education explicitly highlights this connection in its presentation of value change, referring to Ronald Inglehart's thesis that new themes gain in significance where basic needs are largely secured. Moralisation under these conditions is not initially necessarily an expression of hypocrisy, but can be understood as a consequence of social security and cultural modernisation.

Symbolic Boundaries and Moral Distinction

However, this still does not explain why moral language in privileged milieus frequently takes on a demarcating character. Here, research on symbolic boundaries helps. Michèle Lamont and Virág Molnár describe symbolic boundaries as distinctions through which people classify and evaluate practices, groups, and ways of life. Such boundaries structure belonging, recognition, and exclusion; they are thus closely linked to social inequality. Within this framework, moral language can be more than a normative orientation: it can also function as a medium of social distinction. Those who master the "correct" terms, attitudes, and cultural codes signal belonging to a more highly valued milieu.

Status Logics and "Moral Grandstanding"

Against this backdrop, the thesis of a hypermoralising upper class appears socially scientifically plausible, provided it is not formulated essentialistically. The point is not that privileged actors "really" have no moral convictions. The point is rather that moral positions in high-status milieus are frequently doubly functional: they articulate normative convictions and simultaneously generate cultural prestige. Research on "moral grandstanding" provides an illuminating pointer here. Grubbs and colleagues describe this as moral self-presentation for status motives; in their study, such motivation was associated with status-related personality traits and with greater moral-political conflict. The finding comes from the American context and should not be transferred directly to Germany. But it does support the more general assumption that morality in the public sphere can also be status work.

The Compensation Hypothesis

At this point, the assumption of a compensation mechanism gains plausibility. Methodologically, it would be impermissible to impute blanket guilt-deflection or unconscious motives to an entire social group. What is plausible, however, is the weaker assumption that in materially privileged milieus there is an incentive to frame and legitimate one's own social position morally. The more clearly the distance between one's own standard of living and the constraints of others stands out, the more attractive a symbolic self-interpretation may become in which one's own milieu appears not only as successful, but also as particularly reflective, responsible, and sensitive. Morality would then not abolish privilege, but would normatively recode it.

Inheritances, Origins, and Legitimation

For Germany, this hypothesis gains additional plausibility from the structure of wealth accumulation. The German Council of Economic Experts points out that the share of wealth in Germany attributable to inheritances and gifts is estimated at 30 to 50%. If economic security and social status are to a considerable degree also shaped by intergenerational transfers, the need for cultural and normative forms of legitimation increases. Put differently: where social positions are not exclusively the result of one's own achievements, but also rest on origins, family, and institutional advantages, the moral loading of one's own lifestyle as a form of justification becomes more probable. This does not prove a compensation mechanism, but makes the hypothesis analytically comprehensible.

Political Explosiveness

This dynamic becomes particularly politically explosive because it meets a society with real distributional and recognition conflicts. The WSI emphasises in its distributional report that income inequality and poverty in Germany have increased significantly since 2010 and that concerns about one's own standard of living radiate into the middle class. Under such conditions, a discourse that focuses strongly on attitude, language, and symbolic sensitivity can be perceived by lower-income groups as condescending or socially blind. The irritation does not then arise from moral concerns being expressed, but from the fact that such concerns are frequently formulated from positions of relative material security.

Conclusion

The analytical point is therefore not to banish morality from politics. Democracies depend on normative concepts such as justice, responsibility, and dignity. Moralisation becomes problematic where it replaces or overlays material politics. When debates about symbolic correctness, cultural sensitivity, and the right language dominate, while concentration of ownership, low wages, wealth inheritance, and poverty risks less frequently receive the same moral seriousness, a disproportion arises. The thesis developed here remains deliberately interpretive. It does not say that privileged milieus are merely performing their values. It says rather that in a society of high material inequality, moral discourses find it particularly easy to intertwine with distinction, status-securing, and symbolic self-legitimation.

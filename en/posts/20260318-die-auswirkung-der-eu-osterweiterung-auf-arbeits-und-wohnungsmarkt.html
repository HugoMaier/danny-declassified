EU Eastern Enlargement, the Labour Market, and Home Ownership in Germany

An analysis of winners, losers, and social consequences

Core argument: EU Eastern Enlargement was not a blanket job-destroyer for Germany, but it did increase pressure on lower and lower-middle labour market segments and intensified the sense of social blockage when it comes to acquiring property.

If one seriously analyses EU Eastern Enlargement, two channels must be distinguished: labour mobility and trade/offshoring effects. For Germany, research does not produce a simple picture of mass displacement of native workers. The IAB finds no systematic effect of immigration from the new EU member states on benefit dependency (SGB II) among native workers; nor can net emigration of the native population from affected regions, triggered by this migration, be demonstrated. The Bundesbank arrives at a similar basic pattern for the free movement phase since 2011: EU immigration, particularly from central and eastern European accession states, initially dampened wage growth, but had a durably positive effect on the employment of German nationals and did not, according to the study, lead to displacement of resident workers.

The losers were therefore to be found less among "the Germans in general" than within certain segments of the German labour market. Those most under pressure were workers with low incomes, simple or routine tasks, and little bargaining power. The Bundesbank states explicitly that the wage-dampening effects of EU immigration were particularly strong in the lower income groups. The picture becomes even clearer with offshoring to Eastern Europe: one IAB study for German manufacturing finds that offshoring to Eastern Europe was associated with a net wage increase of 4.2% for high-complexity jobs, but a wage decrease of 3.9% for low-complexity jobs. A further study on the employment effects of Eastern Enlargement also shows that firms which had increased their share of imported intermediate inputs before the enlargement were subsequently 0.6 percentage points more negatively affected in terms of employment growth. Translated: the most vulnerable were helper and basic skilled-worker occupations in import-exposed or competition-exposed areas, as well as workers without significant financial reserves, qualification upgrades, or collective bargaining power.

Looking specifically at occupations, the pressure zones lay above all where Eastern European workers were particularly strongly represented or where German firms sourced cheaper intermediate goods and components from Eastern Europe. The IAB shows, for cross-border commuters from Eastern Europe after full freedom of movement since 2011, concentrations among vehicle drivers, logistics helpers, machinery/vehicle technology workers, metal workers, and helper occupations in construction and agriculture. At the same time, many of these occupations had above-average vacancy periods; in 2023 they were particularly high among assistants in general construction and HGV drivers. The conclusion: Eastern Enlargement did not primarily generate broad unemployment in Germany, but rather intensified wage and working-conditions pressure in the lower segment, while at the same time cushioning shortages in some occupations. Most affected were therefore native low-paid helpers, agency workers, temporary employees, and workers in subcontracting chains in construction, logistics, agriculture, and simple industrial activities. This is more a story of polarisation than of a general labour market collapse.

On construction and housing, an important distinction must be made: EU Eastern Enlargement is probably not the main cause of high construction costs in Germany. The data suggest it actually had a net cost-reducing effect, because additional workers became available in bottleneck occupations — a well-founded inference given that Eastern European cross-border commuters work precisely in construction and shortage occupations where filling vacancies takes particularly long. The larger cost driver in recent years has instead been general construction price inflation, materials and finishing costs; Destatis reported a year-on-year increase of 3.2% for new conventional residential buildings in November 2025. On the demand side, however, immigration does genuinely affect rents and to some extent purchase prices: one German study covering 382 districts from 2004 to 2020 finds that an additional international influx of 1% of the initial population raises housing prices short-term by up to 3% and rents by around 1%, though it found no significant effect on house prices. For Eastern Enlargement, this means: it probably made housing more expensive in strong regions primarily through additional demand rather than higher construction costs.

The fact that property has become increasingly out of reach for ordinary earners is therefore primarily a function of the combination of insufficient supply, the concentration of good jobs in metropolitan areas, and already high price levels. The BBSR projects household numbers rising to 42.6 million by 2030 and a need for around 320,000 new homes per year, particularly in economically strong centres. Meanwhile, the house price index stood at 148.0 in 2024 (2015 = 100), the building land price index was already at 187.5 in 2021, and housing costs consumed on average 24.5% of available household income in 2024; 12.0% of the population were considered housing-cost-overburdened, spending more than 40% of their income on housing. Germany also remains with 52.8% renters in 2024 the EU's number-one renting nation. This means: for households without an inheritance, a substantial deposit, or a dual-income model, ownership has become significantly harder to attain — above all in and around growth regions.

The social consequence is, in my view, the real core of the problem. The OECD shows that the German middle class shrank between 1995 and 2018: the share of middle incomes fell from 70% to 64%. Upward mobility has become particularly difficult for young people, people without tertiary education, people in eastern Germany, immigrants, and workers in "typical" middle-class occupations. At the same time, the labour market has polarised since the 1990s; particularly at risk of automation, according to the OECD, among middle incomes are drivers, mobile plant operators, workers in construction, production, and transport, and office support roles. If precisely these groups also experience property — the classic route to wealth accumulation — as moving out of reach, the feeling arises: you work, but structurally you no longer get ahead. It is from exactly this that status anxiety, resentment of open markets and EU free movement, and political susceptibility to parties promising protection, borders, and national priority grow.

My conclusion is therefore: in Germany, it was not "all workers" who suffered under EU Eastern Enlargement, but the lower and lower-middle segments in direct competition with cheaper labour or outsourced value-creation stages — simple industrial, construction, logistics, transport, and agriculture-adjacent activities, often with helper status, agency work, or weak collective bargaining ties. For construction costs, Eastern Enlargement was probably not the main price driver; there, land, undersupply, metropolitan concentration, and general construction price inflation were more important. In housing, Eastern Enlargement probably worked more through additional demand in prospering regions. The social consequence is a sharper divide between asset-owners and non-owners, between winning regions and losing regions — and a growing feeling that social mobility without starting capital is barely worth the effort any more.

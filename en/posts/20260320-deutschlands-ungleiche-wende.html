The AfD did not grow out of one grievance alone. Its rise was prepared by a longer social shift: high unemployment, weaker status security after Agenda 2010, modest but real housing strain, the sale of public housing, and persistent east-west gaps in wealth, wages, and recognition. The East gave this politics its sharpest form; the West is increasingly giving it broader reach.

When Germans remember the early 2000s, they often think first of the euro cash changeover and the feeling that everyday life had become more expensive. But the deeper transformation was social rather than merely monetary. Germany did not become a mass homeowners' society; it remained largely a country of tenants. According to Destatis, the owner-occupied dwelling rate was 40.9% in 1998, 42.6% in 2002, and 41.6% in 2006. At the same time, average gross cold rent rose to 408 euro in 2002 and 410 euro in 2006. The Bundesbank later described German house prices in that decade as showing only marginal fluctuations. The problem, then, was not a classic national property bubble. It was the combination of limited access to ownership, moderate rent pressure, and weakening public buffers.

Those buffers were weakened further by the sale of public and quasi-public housing. The Bundestag's Scientific Services noted that, for housing privatization, the key systematic transaction data came from the Federal Institute for Research on Building, Urban Affairs and Spatial Development rather than from Destatis. Large public sales became emblematic of the period. Dresden's sale of WOBA in 2006 is one of the clearest cases, and the city's own documents record a purchase price of 981.7 million euro. In Lower Saxony, parliamentary documents later described the former state-owned NILEG as having had more than 30,000 dwellings before its sale. Sociologically, this mattered because housing ceased to be seen primarily as a social buffer and was increasingly treated as a financial asset.

Why 1997–2006 matters for the later rise of the AfD

The years before and after the euro changeover also coincided with heavy labour-market strain. Destatis shows unemployment in Germany at 11.4% in 1997, 9.4% in 2001, 11.7% in 2005, and 11.0% in 2006. In the new Länder including Berlin, the rates were far higher. This was the background to Agenda 2010. The Bundestag's own retrospective states that the aim was to raise employment and lower labor costs in order to make Germany more competitive. It lists easier rules for temporary agency work and minor employment, shorter durations of some unemployment benefits, and the merger of unemployment assistance and social assistance under a stronger Fördern und Fordern logic.

This did not simply modernize the labor market. It also changed how social risk was experienced. Research cited by the Hans-Böckler-Stiftung links anti-democratic and far-right attitudes to social disintegration: fears about living standards, old-age security, job futures, lack of voice at work, and the feeling that one's effort is not properly recognized. In that sense, the social turn of 1997–2006 matters as the prehistory of later right-wing populist mobilization. It did not automatically produce the AfD, but it thinned the sense of protection that had once stabilized broad parts of the lower and lower-middle social world.

The electoral rise of the AfD

At federal level, the AfD's rise is clear. The Federal Returning Officer records 4.7% of second votes in the 2013 Bundestag election, 12.6% in 2017, 10.4% in 2021, and 20.8% in the 2025 Bundestag election. This is not a smooth upward line, but it is unmistakably a long-term expansion from near-parliamentary failure to a mass party of protest.

The East is where this rise became socially entrenched first. In 2025, the AfD climbed to 38.6% in Thuringia and 32.5% in Brandenburg. Yet the West can no longer be treated as politically separate. In Bavaria, the party reached 19.0% in 2025. The east-west difference is still strong, but the West is increasingly reproducing the same political logic under different conditions.

East and West: differences that still matter

The strongest east-west differences today are not only attitudinal; they are also material. Destatis reports that full-time employees in the western Länder earned an average of 4,810 euro per month in 2024, compared with 3,973 euro in the eastern Länder. The federal government's 2024 unity report adds that owner-occupiers in eastern Germany typically hold much less net real-estate wealth than those in western Germany: around 100,000 to 150,000 euro in the East versus 250,000 to 350,000 euro in several western Länder. Put simply, the East still has less asset cushioning and less access to rent-producing property income.

There is also a recognition gap. A bpb contribution on eastern underrepresentation argues that East Germans experienced clear marginalization in elite recruitment after unification, and that improvement has only been partial since. The feeling of being treated as a "second-class citizen" remains much more widespread in the East. These are not minor symbolic injuries. They affect whether people see institutions as theirs.

East and West: similarities that are often overlooked

Still, the East and West should not be turned into two separate moral worlds. The bpb's long-run review of political attitudes concludes that the overall direction of development in East and West has been mostly convergent, and that many shifts in public mood happen in broadly similar ways in both parts of the country. That matters because it shows the AfD cannot be explained by an allegedly defective eastern political culture alone.

The similarity lies in the mechanism: insecurity, weak representation, blocked mobility, and cultural conflict can reinforce one another in both East and West. The East is the sharper case because these pressures were layered onto the rupture of 1989–90, the loss of industrial worlds, and the long experience of being governed by largely western elites. But the West now shows comparable syndromes in ex-industrial regions, precarious labour markets, and lower-middle milieus that feel overlooked by established parties.

A sociological reading

The most convincing sociological explanation is cumulative. Material insecurity weakens trust. Weak trust makes representation gaps more painful. Representation gaps make migration and cultural conflicts more combustible. And once these conflicts become the dominant language of politics, they feed back into a harsher social climate. The FGZ's 2025 regional analysis of the German Social Cohesion Panel found that in constituencies with high AfD vote shares, people differentiate more strongly in their sympathies toward cultural, regional, and socio-demographic groups. This points to a broader regional climate of social division, not only to the attitudes of AfD voters themselves.

That is also why it is misleading to choose between "economics" and "culture." The AfD's rise was not caused by economics alone, but economics helped create the field in which its messages could resonate. Nor was it caused by culture alone, because cultural polarization usually bites hardest where insecurity and mistrust are already high. The East gave this syndrome its deepest historical form, but the West increasingly supplies analogous settings: insecure work, housing strain, mistrust of established parties, and the feeling that ordinary effort no longer guarantees security or recognition.

Conclusion

The best way to understand the AfD's rise is to connect the long social turn of 1997–2006 with the later east-west political pattern. Germany's social model did not collapse in one moment, but it became thinner: housing was privatized more than democratized, ownership remained limited, unemployment stayed high long enough to legitimize harsh labour-market reforms, and public security became more conditional. The East lived these changes more sharply and with deeper historical memory; the West is increasingly living their political consequences.

The East-West divide is real, but it is not a divide between one wounded region and one healthy one. It is a difference of intensity within a broader German story. East Germany gave the AfD its deepest soil. West Germany is increasingly giving it broader territory.

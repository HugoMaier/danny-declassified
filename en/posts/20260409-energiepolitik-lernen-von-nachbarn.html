Introduction

We Germans tend, when it comes to energy policy, either to engage in a purely German navel-gazing exercise or, unfortunately, far too often to display the unpleasant habit of lecturing our neighbors.

Neither is helpful. Precisely the comparison with Great Britain, France, and Italy shows that Germany is neither hopelessly behind in energy policy nor automatically the benchmark for everyone else. Each of these countries has made mistakes, each has developed strengths, and it is exactly from this mixture that more can be learned than from any national self-congratulation.

Institutions Beat Symbolism

The first lesson from abroad is this: good energy policy is not only technology policy, but above all institutional policy. Great Britain is the best example of this. With the Climate Change Act, the carbon budgets, and the independent Climate Change Committee, the country has created a framework that makes political goals binding and keeps governments under constant pressure to justify their actions. The fact that the United Kingdom has met its first three carbon budgets and roughly halved its emissions since 1990 is no miracle; it is the result of an architecture that makes policy reversals harder and accountability more visible. Germany also has ambitious goals, but the IEA explicitly calls here for greater long-term political stability, integrated planning, and faster permitting procedures. That is precisely where an uncomfortable point lies: we often debate goals passionately, but far too little the institutional form in which they can be implemented reliably.

France's Lesson: Price and Reliability Matter

The second lesson comes from France: in the end, what matters is not the moral purity of an energy mix, but whether a country can provide electricity reliably, cleanly, and at bearable costs. In 2024, France crossed the threshold for the first time at which more than 95 percent of its electricity generation came from low-carbon sources; at the same time, the average French spot price in 2024 stood at 58 euros per MWh. That does not mean France is a perfect model. The country has delays, technical problems, and high nuclear investment risks. But it shows something that is too often suppressed in Germany: inexpensive and relatively predictable low-carbon electricity is not only climate policy, but also industrial policy, social policy, and location policy. Anyone who tries to pit energy policy against economic policy has already lost.

This is precisely where a German weakness becomes obvious. According to Eurostat, Germany recorded the highest household electricity prices in the EU in the second half of 2024, at 39.43 euros per 100 kWh. At the same time, the KfW Energy Transition Barometer 2025 shows continued strong support for the energy transition, but also a declining willingness to act: 83 percent consider the energy transition important, but only 59 percent show a high willingness to take action themselves. That is politically highly relevant. People and businesses can support major transformations, but not indefinitely if the system is perceived as expensive, confusing, and unfair. The German debate tends to treat high ambition as a moral success in itself. Our neighbors remind us that acceptance is not measured in Sunday speeches, but in bills, procedures, and visible results.

Britain's Strength: Clear Frameworks, Clear Signals

There is something else Germany can learn from Great Britain: market mechanisms work well when they are embedded in a credible political framework. With CfDs, carbon budgets, and independent performance monitoring, the British system has created a link between market and state that gives investors direction without centrally commanding every single step. The fact that public support for renewable energy still stood at 78 percent in winter 2025, despite the severe energy crisis there as well, speaks to a remarkable political resilience. Germany, by contrast, often argues simultaneously about technology pathways, responsibilities, levies, grids, construction timelines, and subsidy rules. The result is often not creative openness, but strategic unrest.

Italy's Lesson: Pragmatism and Infrastructure

Italy, in turn, is an underrated teacher in another discipline: pragmatism under difficult conditions. The country has traditionally been heavily dependent on gas and is certainly no model of an ideal system. But after 2022, Italy rapidly diversified its supply sources, used its Mediterranean location to geopolitical energy advantage, and according to the IEA is broadly on track with the emissions and efficiency targets of its NECP. At the same time, it remains clearly visible there how damaging slow permitting and infrastructure bottlenecks can be. Germany can learn from Italy that security of supply does not emerge from abstract lists of goals, but through ports, pipelines, storage, grids, and accelerated procedures. One does not have to copy every Italian solution to acknowledge that, in the crisis, Italy often acted more soberly and more flexibly than German debate tends to admit.

Germany's Strength – and Germany's Weakness

This does not mean that Germany should only learn and others only teach. On the contrary: Germany has genuine strengths of its own. Public support for the energy transition is high, the expansion of renewable energy has developed strong momentum, and the German transformation debate is more broadly rooted in society than in many other countries. But that is exactly why Germany should allow itself a more sober view. A country that produces high prices, slow procedures, and unclear signals cannot console itself with moral self-satisfaction alone. The IEA puts it diplomatically: Germany needs long-term political stability, integrated planning, infrastructure development, and faster permitting in order to advance its energy transition successfully. Less diplomatically put: we need more state capacity in organization and less symbolism in self-presentation.

Conclusion

Perhaps the most important lesson from the comparison with France, Great Britain, and Italy is this: Germany should stop treating energy policy as a moral identity project and start organizing it consistently as a strategic task of the state. Great Britain shows how binding institutions create planning certainty. France shows how important cheap and low-carbon electricity is for prosperity and industry. Italy shows that infrastructure, diversification, and administrative pragmatism make the difference, especially in times of crisis.

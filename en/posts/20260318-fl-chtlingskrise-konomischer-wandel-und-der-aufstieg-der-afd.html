The Refugee Crisis, Economic Change, and the Rise of the AfD

How globalisation, EU Eastern Enlargement, and the turning point of 2015/16 changed the political landscape in Germany

18 March 2026

The refugee crisis of 2015/16 was not an isolated event for Germany, but a political accelerant in a society that was already under considerable economic and social adaptation pressure. Since the late 1990s, the economic structure had shifted markedly from manufacturing to services. For many workers in the industrial and lower-middle segment, this meant not simply progress, but greater insecurity, more competition, and weaker expectations of upward mobility. Globalisation and EU Eastern Enlargement created prosperity overall, but their costs were concentrated on certain groups: routinised industrial workers, those in precarious employment, structurally weaker regions, and younger people without assets.

Against this backdrop, the refugee crisis struck an already irritated social state. The Federal Office for Migration and Refugees (BAMF) registered 476,649 asylum applications in 2015 and as many as 745,545 in 2016. The Federal Agency for Civic Education described the situation at the time as an administrative and infrastructure crisis. What was politically effective was not just the absolute number of refugees, but the impression that the state and municipalities could only partially manage the situation. Where housing, schools, administration, police, or the housing market were already under strain, migration quickly became a visible symbol of a more comprehensive loss of control.

The AfD benefited from this in an already prepared political space. Originally, it started primarily as a euro-critical party. But the Federal Agency for Civic Education explicitly notes that the refugee crisis that set in from September 2015 sent the party sharply upward in polls and delivered record results in state elections in 2016. In 2017, the AfD entered the Bundestag for the first time with 12.6%; by 2025 it had already reached 20.8%. The refugee crisis was therefore not the sole origin of its rise, but it was the phase in which a previously rather diffuse mood of protest translated into a stable political bloc.

So why are so many workers moving to the AfD? The simplest answer — "only because of migration" — is too short. The data instead point to an overlay of social insecurity, status anxiety, political alienation, and cultural conflict. The Friedrich Ebert Foundation reports for the 2025 federal election that the AfD received 38% among workers, putting it clearly ahead of CDU/CSU and SPD. Among voters who rated their own economic situation as "not so good" or "bad," it reached as high as 39%. This shows: the AfD is strongest where economic vulnerability and pessimistic expectations of the future coincide.

Labour sociology research also fits precisely with this finding. The Hans Böckler Foundation shows that workers and job seekers are overrepresented in the AfD's voter base. This group reports more frequently on insecure jobs, wages that are too low, a lack of recognition, less co-determination at work, and lower satisfaction with employee representation. Migration is a central issue for these voters, but it is embedded in the feeling that their own lifetime achievement is losing value and that political institutions respond more to other groups than to the working middle. The AfD can build a simple narrative from this: you follow the rules, you work hard, and yet the elites no longer take care of you first.

This is precisely why the refugee crisis was so powerful. It made abstract fears concretely visible. Globalisation and EU Eastern Enlargement had already previously intensified competitive pressure, wage suppression, and regional inequalities. But these processes were often technical, long-term, and difficult to grasp. The migration dynamics of 2015/16 were, by contrast, immediately visible: at railway stations, in municipalities, in school classes, in housing facilities, and in political debates. They condensed many existing concerns into a single, emotionally charged conflict theme. For many people, migration thus became a cipher for something larger: for insufficient state capacity to act, for unfairly distributed burdens, and for the impression that their own group is losing priority.

The housing and ownership question adds another dimension. Germany remains the EU's number-one renting nation, and a significant share of the population is overburdened by housing costs. When people simultaneously experience jobs becoming more insecure, wealth accumulation becoming more difficult, and competition for scarce housing increasing, economic change fuses with cultural insecurity. The refugee crisis was also politically effective because in many people's minds it was linked to already strained housing markets, overburdened municipalities, and the feeling of dwindling public resources — regardless of how strong individual statistical effects actually were in detail.

Crucially, the effect of the refugee crisis lay less in simple material displacement than in a crisis of representation. The bpb points out that right-wing populist parties, particularly for people from the working and lower classes, generate the feeling that they represent their interests better than other parties. This is precisely where a central problem of the established parties lies: many workers experience intensive public debate about migration, climate, Europe, or urban lifestyle issues, while their own questions about wages, recognition, social security, housing, and order are answered too late, too technocratically, or not at all. The AfD fills this void politically — even if its concrete economic and social policy often contradicts the material interests of many workers.

My conclusion is therefore: the refugee crisis did not change the political landscape in Germany out of nowhere, but dramatically accelerated an already ongoing structural conflict. Globalisation and EU Eastern Enlargement had already produced winners and losers. The crisis of 2015/16 turned this into a visible confrontation over belonging, control, distribution, and fairness. Many workers are moving to the AfD because in their voting decision economic insecurity, blocked upward mobility, status loss, and alienation from the established parties all converge. Anyone who wants to reverse this trend must therefore do more than just debate migration policy. They must renew the social promise that work creates recognition, housing remains affordable, and the state does not leave its citizens with the feeling of being mere spectators in their own society.

How Germany Could Stabilise Its Society and Become More Strategically Independent

Essay

Germany today faces a dual challenge: it must calm its society socially and at the same time reduce its strategic vulnerability. The two belong together. Political radicalisation does not arise only from cultural conflicts, but also where people gain the impression that work, housing, and security for the future are no longer reliably organised. At the same time, the European debate on energy, medicines, raw materials, and industry shows that open markets bring prosperity but, without resilience, also create new dependencies. Germany is not starting from scratch: manufacturing remains strong and continues to account for a higher share of gross value added than the EU average. But social tensions in the housing market and strategic bottlenecks in critical supply chains make clear that the status quo is not enough.

The first lever for stabilisation is housing. As long as large parts of the population feel that — despite working — they can no longer find a secure place in the prospering regions of the country, any political calming will remain superficial. Germany, with 52.8% renters, remains the EU's number-one renting nation, and the BBSR sees a need for around 320,000 new homes per year until 2030. In reality, only around 251,900 homes were completed in 2024. This gap is not only a housing policy problem, but a problem of social order. Those who can barely pay rent, or who experience home ownership as unattainable, lose more easily the trust in society's promise of upward mobility. Housing must therefore be treated politically like a form of critical infrastructure: with faster planning approvals, more serial construction, stronger municipal land policy, and a significantly larger sector of price-capped and social housing.

The second lever is material security for the working middle class. The OECD shows that the German middle class shrank from 70% to 64% of the population between 1995 and 2018. This is politically so significant because democracies depend on the feeling that effort, qualifications, and following the rules ultimately lead to stability. Where this feeling erodes, the willingness to listen to radical political offers grows. Germany therefore needs not just more growth, but a visibly fairer distribution of security: stronger collective bargaining coverage, less abuse of contractor and subcontracting chains, better retraining in industrial transition, and targeted regional policy where structural ruptures hit hardest. Societal stability does not arise through appeals, but through reliable everyday conditions.

The third lever is energy. Since the break with Russia, Europe has diversified its energy supply, but built new dependencies. The European Commission itself emphasised in March 2026 that Europe's energy independence must be further strengthened and costs reduced. This shows: the task is not done. Germany therefore needs an energy policy that thinks not only in climate terms, but also in industrial and security-policy terms. More grids, more storage, faster planning approvals, more renewable generation, reliable back-up capacities, and lower electricity costs for energy-intensive industry would be not only economic policy, but a form of national and European resilience. A country that feels permanently exposed in terms of energy supply and prices will also become politically nervous.

A fourth step would be to selectively repatriate only truly critical supply chains to European hands. Full autarky would be expensive and unrealistic. But the EU is itself now reacting to the fact that certain dependencies have grown too large. The Critical Medicines Act, proposed by the Commission in March 2025, aims to improve the availability and production of critical medicines in the EU and to make supply chains more robust. The Critical Raw Materials Act sets, among other goals, a target by 2030 that no more than 65% of consumption of a strategic raw material should come from a single third country. That is the right approach: not reshoring everything, but building European capacities precisely in medicines, certain active ingredients, raw materials, semiconductor components, grid technology, and defence goods — wherever outages would jeopardise the state's capacity to act.

This also implies that Germany needs a modern industrial policy, but not a nostalgic one. The goal is not to artificially preserve past industrial structures, but to secure the next industrial base in Europe. The fact that the Commission proposed new measures to strengthen European industry at the start of March 2026 shows how strongly this view has entered the mainstream. A smart German strategy would therefore focus on future fields such as grid technology, power electronics, batteries, medical technology, robotics, AI in industry, and defence-adjacent dual-use technologies. Public procurement, simpler financing, and faster procedures could achieve more here than grand speeches about competitiveness. Strategic independence does not mean closure, but the capacity to still act autonomously even under stress.

In the end, however, the most important political task may be a different one: making statehood credible again. A stable society needs not only homes, energy, and industry, but also the feeling that administration, infrastructure, schools, courts, and municipalities function. On the subject of migration in particular, this would be decisive: not symbolic politics, but the visible combination of order, control, and integration. When the state demonstrates control while also delivering services, the attractiveness of parties that thrive on loss of control and institutional weakness also diminishes. Germany's problem is therefore not only economic. It is a trust problem. And trust grows where politics makes everyday life predictable again.

My conclusion is therefore simple: Germany will become socially more stable and strategically more independent if it manages three things simultaneously — affordable housing, reliable opportunities for upward mobility for the working middle, and less dependency on the few truly critical goods. That would not be a return to a closed economy, nor a romantic dream of national autarky. It would be a pragmatic reordering of Germany's social contract under the conditions of a more insecure world.

Our research has repeatedly shown one pattern: Europe often makes two mistakes at once when it comes to China. The first is moral self-soothing — telling itself that China's successes are based only on authoritarianism and are therefore irrelevant for Germany or the EU. The second is the opposite extreme: seeing only efficiency while ignoring how much of that efficiency is supported by state-driven market distortions, opacity, and strategic dependencies. Both views are misleading. The sober answer is this: yes, Germany and the EU can learn from China — but they should not copy the unfair parts.

The key point, then, is to distinguish between legitimate lessons and dangerous imitation. Legitimate learning means better execution, more consistent scaling, more strategic industrial policy, more user-friendly digitalization, and more piloting before a full rollout. Dangerous imitation would mean state-distorted financing, politically driven overcapacity, lack of transparency, and new dependencies. That is exactly the line that should guide both Germany's and the EU's approach to China.

1. Industrial Policy: China Thinks in Systems, Europe Often Thinks in Isolated Measures

China is not merely fast in key future industries — it is systematic. The Draghi report describes the pressure on Europe in clean tech and electric mobility explicitly as a consequence of massive industrial policy, rapid innovation, control over raw materials, and the ability to produce on a continent-wide scale. The IEA adds the hard numbers: in 2024, China accounted for more than 70 percent of global electric vehicle production and 80 percent of global battery cell production; around 85 percent of global battery manufacturing capacity was located in China. That is no longer just an advantage — it is a position of industrial power.

What Germany and the EU should learn from this is not subsidy romanticism, but system thinking. Europe still treats decarbonization, industry, energy prices, grids, demand, and production build-up too often as separate policy fields. China treats them as one connected industrial chain. That is one of its real strengths.

Concrete proposal for Germany: Germany should adopt a ten-year scaling pact for three to five key sectors — for example batteries, power electronics, grid infrastructure, heat pumps, and industrial software — each with secured demand through public and private offtake agreements, accelerated permitting, standardized grid and site procedures, and a clear KfW financing track for the transition from pilot to mass production. That would not be central planning. It would simply be strategic industrial policy at last.

Concrete proposal for the EU: The EU should create joint demand instruments, more coordinated support, and cross-border production clusters for the same key sectors. Draghi explicitly argues for a more coherent European industrial policy. In practice, that means fewer small national programs and more shared scaling capacity.

2. Infrastructure: China Plans for the Long Term — Germany Often Replans Too Often

One of the most underestimated Chinese lessons lies in infrastructure organization. The World Bank report on China's high-speed rail describes China's experience explicitly as a source of "key lessons" for other countries. The interesting point is not only the sheer size of the network, but the combination of long-term planning, institutional continuity, standardization, and execution discipline.

Germany has a structural problem here. It is not lacking studies, commissions, or good intentions — it lacks prioritization and staying power. Infrastructure projects in Germany are frequently redesigned, reprioritized, or worn down over years between the federal level, the states, project sponsors, and procedures. China shows how much difference it makes when a state defines corridors once and then actually follows through on them over many years. That does not mean abolishing participation rights. It means combining better planning with more reliable implementation.

Concrete proposal for Germany: A federal infrastructure law with a small number of legally prioritized corridors for rail, power grids, industrial connections, and port hinterland links — each with fixed timeframes, standardized planning documents, and centralized project management. Germany's weakness is not a lack of goals, but a lack of implementation architecture.

Concrete proposal for the EU: The EU should manage its major transport, energy, and industrial corridors more like genuine European projects, with standard contracts, fixed milestones, and delivery monitoring that identifies delays continuously rather than only at the end.

3. Digitalization: China Builds from the User's Perspective, Germany Often Builds from the Form

China also offers a real lesson in everyday digitalization. BIS findings on QR-based digital payment systems in China show how strongly simple, cheap, and mass-usable digital infrastructures can accelerate adoption. The benefit lay not only in payment itself, but in the link to credit, data flows, and everyday services. Europe's problem is often not a lack of technology, but a lack of consistency in user-centered design.

Germany in particular performs poorly here. The EU Commission's 2025 Digital Decade report says explicitly that Germany needs to improve digital public services, digital skills, and high-capacity networks. FTTP covers only a fraction of households and stands at about half the EU average. In addition, eID use in Germany remains significantly below the EU average, meaning that the public sector still depends too heavily on physical processes.

Concrete proposal for Germany: One single digital citizen account, a mandatory interoperable eID, a consistent once-only principle, and integrated payment functions directly inside administrative processes. Not twenty half-digital portals, but one logic: one access point, one data record, one procedure. This is precisely where Germany could learn from China's user-centered approach without copying its surveillance state.

Concrete proposal for the EU: The EU should not only regulate the digital wallet and cross-border digital identity, but operationally standardize them for company formation, customs procedures, health data, and skilled migration. Europe's strength ought to be interoperability; so far it is too often merely a stated goal.

4. Reform Policy: China Often Tests First — Europe Rolls Out Too Broadly Too Early

One particularly smart Chinese method does not belong to just one policy field, but to the way reforms are introduced. A World Bank contribution on China's reform path describes the approach as pragmatic and systematic: pilot, evaluate results, scale what works, discard what does not. That is a strength of what one might call a learning state.

Germany and the EU often do the opposite. They try to write highly complex rules as completely as possible from the start, only to discover the practical problems once full implementation begins. Especially in areas such as the energy transition, housing, digitalization, industrial policy, or labor market integration, a more consistent culture of piloting and evaluation would often be better than immediate nationwide perfection-by-legislation.

Concrete proposal for Germany: Every major reform project should include a mandatory pilot phase in several states or regions, an external evaluation, and a sunset clause for ineffective elements. Regulatory sandboxes should move from being the exception to being the rule.

Concrete proposal for the EU: In selected single-market and innovation fields, the EU should rely more on regulated sandboxes and phased rollouts instead of forcing final uniformity too early. Europe needs more procedural learning capacity, not just greater ambition in its goals.

5. Innovation: Europe Invents a Great Deal, China Industrializes Faster

Here too, China's strength is less mysterious than it is often portrayed. China links research, financing, manufacturing, and the domestic market more tightly than Europe does. In electric mobility this is clearly visible: China does not only produce a lot, it also drives down costs through scale, competition, and supply-chain integration. The IEA shows that in 2024 the average prices of battery electric vehicles in Germany were still around 20 percent above comparable combustion cars, while China in many segments is already much closer to — or even below — cost parity.

For Germany and the EU, the lesson is clear: research alone is not enough. Whoever leaves the industrial learning curve to others ultimately loses not just production, but often the next cycle of innovation as well. That is why Draghi rightly describes Europe's problem as one of fragmentation and a lack of coherent strategy.

Concrete proposal for Germany: A national program for pilot and pre-series manufacturing in key industries, tied to public procurement and private co-financing. Germany often funds research and then complains that the factory gets built elsewhere. What is missing is the institutional bridge in between.

Concrete proposal for the EU: A genuine European scale-up fund plus joint procurement for future technologies. Europe does not only need more venture capital — it also needs more public demand that brings domestic production into serial manufacturing.

6. The Hard Limit: Europe Should Learn Nothing from China's Market Distortion

So much for the legitimate areas of learning. But our research is just as clear on the other side: a considerable part of China's economic force is linked to state-generated market distortions. In the WTO chair's concluding remarks to the 2024 China review, it is noted that members expressed serious skepticism about the transparency and quality of China's subsidy notifications, and that many viewed Chinese subsidies as distortive to global markets and as a driver of overcapacity. The OECD further concluded in 2025 that firms based in China benefited more heavily from below-market borrowing than firms elsewhere.

The consequences are concrete. The European Commission imposed definitive countervailing duties on battery electric vehicles from China because its investigation concluded that the Chinese BEV value chain benefits from unfair state subsidies and that European producers face the threat of economic injury. At the same time, Eurostat reported an EU trade deficit with China of €359.8 billion in 2025. The ECB notes that sectors more exposed to Chinese competition saw stronger declines in job vacancies. On top of that comes the issue of strategic dependency: according to a Commission impact assessment, the EU is almost entirely dependent on imports from China for solar modules, while for solar PV and batteries the combination of overcapacity and dependence on a single source is particularly acute.

That is exactly why the right European lesson is not: "We need to do the same." The right lesson is: we need more strategy without market distortion, more resilience without protectionist closure, and more industrial policy without blindness toward unfair competition.

Concrete proposal for Germany: In public procurement for strategic sectors, Germany should systematically use resilience and reciprocity criteria, align KfW and export instruments more strongly toward diversification, and define minimum shares of alternative sources for critical intermediate goods. German openness has often been naive; it now needs to become more robust.

Concrete proposal for the EU: The EU should use anti-subsidy instruments, investment screening, origin and transparency requirements, and European lead markets much more consistently. Not protectionism for its own sake, but defensible openness. Anyone who wants fair markets must actually sanction unfair practices.

Conclusion

Germany and the EU really can learn something from China. They can learn to prioritize more clearly, scale more decisively, build infrastructure more consistently, test reforms before rolling them out, and design digitalization more from the point of view of everyday life. What they should not learn is state-generated market distortion, opacity, and the willingness to secure economic strength through new forms of dependency. The right conclusion from our research is therefore not "more China." The right conclusion is: more German and European state capacity, but under the rule of law; more strategy, but fair; more speed, but without sacrificing the rules of an open market.
